1. Menisci — Medial and Lateral

Slide 3 – Joints of the knee

Slide 4 – Menisci

The menisci are crescent-shaped fibrocartilaginous structures sitting on the tibial plateau — intracapsular, shock absorbing, and load distributing.

  • Shock absorption: distribute compressive loads across the tibiofemoral joint
  • Load distribution: increase joint congruency
  • Injury risk: susceptible to twisting mechanisms. Medial meniscus more commonly torn (less mobile — tethered to MCL)
Unhappy Triad: ACL + MCL + medial meniscus — from valgus contact injury. Medial meniscus torn due to its MCL attachment.

4. Patellofemoral Joint (PFJ)

Slide 14 – Posterior tibiofemoral / popliteal fossa

Slide 15 – Patella and patellofemoral joint

The patellofemoral joint is the articulation between the posterior patella and the femoral trochlear groove. The patella is the largest sesamoid bone, embedded in the quadriceps tendon.

  • Medial and lateral facets contact the trochlear groove distally
  • Increases quadriceps lever arm by ~30% for knee extension
  • Lateral trochlear groove is more prominent — resists lateral patellar dislocation
PFPS: Anterior knee pain from patellar maltracking. Treat with VMO + hip strengthening, patellar taping, activity modification.

All four insert via the quadriceps tendon → patella → patellar ligament → tibial tuberosity. All innervated by the femoral nerve. Primary knee extensors.

Clinical: Quadriceps weakness is the primary rehab target post-ACL, post-TKR, and in PFPS. VMO is reflexly inhibited by joint effusion — rapid wasting after knee injury is the result.

Medial hamstrings (semitend/semimemb): tibial IR. Biceps femoris: tibial ER. Important for dynamic rotational knee stability.
Hamstring strain: Most common muscle injury in sport. Proximal MTJ of biceps femoris long head during late swing phase of sprinting. Nordic hamstring exercise reduces injury rate by ~50%.
