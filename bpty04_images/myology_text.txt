=== PAGE 5 ===
CARDIAC MUSCLE
• Makes up the wall of the heart: pulsing action to
circulate the blood
❖ Involuntary: controlled by the autonomic nervous
system (not under our conscious control)
→ Do not instruct the muscle fibers in the heart to beat and push
blood through the body
❖ Striated: bands of contractile proteins generating
steady and powerful contractions
5
=== PAGE 6 ===
SMOOTH MUSCLE
•Present in the walls of hollow organs, vessels and respiratory pathways
❖Involuntary: controlled by the autonomic nervous system (not under our
conscious control)
→ In presence of food: smooth muscle automatically generates the
wavelike contractions = peristalsis
→ Within blood vessels and bronchioles: dilates and contracts these
structures to increase or decrease the flow of blood or air
❖Unstriated muscle fibers: contractile proteins are scattered rather than
aligned
→ Not necessary because smooth muscle contractions are slow,
steady, and somewhat weaker than the two other types of muscles

SKELETAL MUSCLE
• Connected to bones and produce
movement at joints
• Around 220 individual muscles
❖ Voluntary (under conscious control):
→ We decide how and when our skeletal
muscles contract to produce movement
❖ Striated: producing very strong, rapid
contractions when activated
❖ Roles: motion; posture; protection;
thermogenesis; vascular pump

SKELETAL MUSCLE TISSUE: Macroscopic anatomy
• MUSCLE FIBERS: individual muscle cells
→ Wrapped in a connective tissue: ENDOMYSIUM

• MUSCLE BELLY: Bundles of bundles of fascicles
→ Enveloped by the EPIMYSIUM
→ All of these layers work together to help
transmit force while protecting the
muscle fibers from damage

PARALLEL ARRANGEMENTS: Fusiform muscles
• Thick central belly
• Tapered end: force production into
concentrated area, usually specific bony
landmarks
• Muscle fibers are arranged parallel
• Line of action runs in a straight line between
the attachment points
Examples: biceps brachii

THE ANATOMICAL PLANES
• The anatomical position:
→ The body is upright standing directly facing the observer
→ Feet flat and directed forward
→ The upper limbs are at the body's sides with the palms facing forward
• The sagittal plane or longitudinal plane
→ Divides the body into right and left
→ Flexion/Extension; Dorsiflexion/Plantarflexion
• The frontal plane or coronal plane
→ Divides the body in anterior (ventral) and posterior (dorsal)
→ Abduction/ Adduction; Inversion/ Eversion
• The transversal plane
→ Divides the body in cranial (superior) and caudal (inferior)
→ Internal/External Rotation; Pronation/Supination

MUSCLE DESCRIPTION: Origin vs Insertion
• ORIGIN:
→ Attachment site that does not move during
contraction
→ Proximal: closer to the body (the knee is proximal
compared to the ankle)
• INSERTION:
→ Attachment site that does move during
contraction
→ Distal, or further away to the body (the ankle is
distal compared to the knee)
• Examples:
→ coracobrachialis: attaching to the coracoid
process of the scapula
→ Iliacus: attached to the iliac fossae of the
pelvis

MUSCLE RELATIONSHIPS
• Muscles work together to achieve some activities
→ Agonist
→ Muscle involved in joint movement
→ Point of reference when describing relationships with other muscles
→ Example: Biceps brachii is the agonist during elbow flexion
→ Antagonist
→ Opposite action of the agonist
→ Example: Triceps brachii is the antagonist during elbow flexion
→ Agonist/ Antagonist relationship is critical for balance, slowing and controlling
movement
→ Synergists
→ Assist in the function of the agonist
→ Stabilizing, steering or contributing at a joint
→ Direct (common actions) vs relative synergists (few action in common)
→ Example: supraspinatus assists the deltoid during shoulder abduction
