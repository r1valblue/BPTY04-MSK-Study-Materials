1. The Sacroiliac Joint (SIJ)

Slide 3 – Sacroiliac joint overview

The sacroiliac joint is the articulation between the sacrum and the auricular surface of the ilium. It is a synovial joint with a C-shaped (or L-shaped) joint surface, primarily responsible for transferring load between the lower limbs and the spinal column.

Key function: Weight transfer from lower limb to spinal column. Normally very little movement — stability is prioritised. Held in place by some of the strongest ligaments in the body.

The SIJ is reinforced by five major ligaments: Sacrotuberous · Sacrospinous · Anterior sacroiliac · Posterior sacroiliac · Interosseous sacroiliac

3. Symphysis Pubis

Slide 8 – Symphysis pubis

Slide 9 – Superior pubic ligament

The symphysis pubis is where the two hip bones articulate anteriorly. It is a secondary cartilaginous joint (symphysis): hyaline cartilage on each surface joined by a fibrocartilaginous interpubic disc. Normally no significant movement — during pregnancy relaxin allows slight separation.

4. The Hip Joint

Slide 11 – Hip joint overview

Slide 12 – Intracapsular ligaments

The hip joint (femoroacetabular joint) is a ball-and-socket (spheroidal) synovial joint between the femoral head and the acetabulum — a deep cup formed by ilium (superior 2/5), ischium (posterior 2/5), and pubis (anterior 1/5).

Acetabular Labrum

  • Fibrocartilaginous rim deepening the socket; creates negative intra-articular pressure
  • Increases surface area for articulation
  • Essential for keeping the femoral head in the socket

5. Hip Extracapsular Ligaments

Slide 13 – Iliofemoral ligament

Slide 14 – Ischiofemoral & pubofemoral ligaments

Three strong extracapsular ligaments spiral around the capsule, all tightening in extension (passive stability for standing) and slackening in flexion.

LigamentOriginInsertionLimits
Iliofemoral (Y-ligament of Bigelow) AIIS + acetabular limbus Intertrochanteric line Strongest ligament in the body. Limits extension + ER
Ischiofemoral Ischium Greater trochanter (medial) Limits IR; stability in extension; lax in flexion
Pubofemoral Superior ramus of pubis Intertrochanteric line Limits extension, ABD, and ER
In flexion + abduction + ER all three ligaments slacken — the hip is least stable in this position (risk of posterior dislocation after THR).
