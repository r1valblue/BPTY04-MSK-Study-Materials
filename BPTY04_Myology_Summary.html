1. Types of Muscle Tissue

There are more than 650 muscles in the human body. Myology is the study of muscles — tissues capable of contraction and relaxation. Muscles are divided into three types based on structure and function:

Slide 4 – Muscle types overview

Slide 5 – Cardiac muscle

Cardiac Muscle

  • Makes up the wall of the heart; produces a pulsing action to circulate blood
  • Involuntary — controlled by the autonomic nervous system (not under conscious control)
  • Striated — bands of contractile proteins generate steady, powerful contractions

Slide 6 – Smooth muscle

Slide 7 – Skeletal muscle

Smooth Muscle

  • Found in walls of hollow organs, blood vessels, and respiratory pathways
  • Involuntary — autonomic nervous system control
  • Generates peristalsis (wavelike contractions) in the GI tract; dilates/constricts blood vessels and bronchioles
  • Unstriated — contractile proteins scattered (not aligned); slower, weaker contractions than other types

Skeletal Muscle

  • Connected to bones; produce movement at joints (~220 individual muscles)
  • Voluntary — under conscious control
  • Striated — strong, rapid contractions when activated
  • Roles: motion; posture; protection; thermogenesis; vascular pump

2. Macroscopic Anatomy of Skeletal Muscle

Slide 8 – Connective tissue wrappings

Slide 9 – Hierarchical structure

Skeletal muscle is organised hierarchically, with connective tissue wrappings at each level that support, protect, and transmit force:

LevelStructureConnective Tissue Wrapping
Cell levelMuscle fibres (individual muscle cells)Endomysium
Bundle levelFascicles (groups of muscle fibres)Perimysium
Whole muscleMuscle belly (bundles of fascicles)Epimysium

Slide 10 – Musculotendinous junction

Tendon & Musculotendinous Junction

  • The epimysium converges distally to form the tendon
  • The musculotendinous junction (MTJ) is the point where the connective tissue layers begin to converge
  • All layers work together to transmit force while protecting muscle fibres from damage

3. Fibre Direction & Arrangements

The direction of muscle fibres determines both the range of motion and the force (power) a muscle can produce. There are two major divisions:

PARALLEL — fibres of equal length that do not intersect a central tendon
PENNATE — feather-shaped; shorter fibres intersect a central tendon at an angle (pennation angle)
