Q1Recall

Name the 8 carpal bones in both rows (proximal then distal, lateral to medial) and give a mnemonic.

Proximal row (lateral → medial): Scaphoid · Lunate · Triquetrum · Pisiform
Distal row (lateral → medial): Trapezium · Trapezoid · Capitate · Hamate

Mnemonic: She Likes To Play / Try To Catch Her
Q2Recall

What structures pass through the carpal tunnel? What nerve is compressed in carpal tunnel syndrome?

Carpal tunnel contents: Flexor digitorum superficialis (4 tendons) · Flexor digitorum profundus (4 tendons) · Flexor pollicis longus (1 tendon) · Median nerve

Note: The flexor carpi radialis runs in a separate tunnel in the trapezium groove, NOT the carpal tunnel. The ulnar nerve travels through Guyon's canal, NOT the carpal tunnel.

Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS): Median nerve compression → paraesthesia in the lateral 3½ digits (thumb, index, middle, and lateral half of ring finger); thenar wasting in severe/chronic cases.
Q3Recall

Describe the TFCC (Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex) — its components and function.

The TFCC is a complex of fibrous and cartilaginous structures on the ulnar side of the wrist:
1. Triangular fibrocartilage disc — bridges the distal radius to the ulnar head; absorbs and transmits forces across the distal radioulnar joint
2. Radioulnar ligaments (dorsal + volar) — stabilise the DRUJ; resist pronation-supination
3. Ulnocarpal ligaments (ulnolunate, ulnotriquetral) — stabilise the ulnar side of the wrist

Function: load transmission across the wrist (20% of axial load crosses the ulnar column via the TFCC); DRUJ stabilisation; cushioning. Tears cause ulnar-sided wrist pain — the 'medial meniscus of the wrist'.
Q4Recall

What is the 1st CMC joint? Why is it said to allow 'opposition'?

The 1st carpometacarpal (CMC) joint is a saddle joint between the trapezium and the base of the 1st metacarpal. Its unique saddle-within-saddle geometry allows:
• Flexion/extension in one plane
• Abduction/adduction in another plane
• Axial rotation (conjunct rotation) — the combination of all three movements = opposition

Opposition brings the thumb pad to face the little finger pad, enabling precision grip — a defining feature of human hand function. No other species has an opposable thumb with this degree of rotational freedom.
Q5Recall

Name the thenar muscles and their actions.

• Abductor pollicis brevis (APB) — palmar abduction of thumb (perpendicular to palm); median nerve
• Flexor pollicis brevis (FPB) — flexion at thumb MCP; median nerve (superficial head) + ulnar nerve (deep head)
• Opponens pollicis — draws thumb metacarpal anteriorly and rotates it into opposition; median nerve

All three are median nerve innervated (primarily recurrent branch of median nerve). All three arise from the flexor retinaculum and adjacent carpal bones. Thenar wasting is the hallmark of severe CTS.
Q6Application

A patient has weakness of finger abduction and cannot cross their fingers. They also have weakness of index finger abduction and 5th finger abduction. Which muscles are affected and which nerve?

Finger abduction is produced by the dorsal interossei (4 muscles: each abducts a digit from the middle finger axis — 1st: index away from middle; 2nd: middle toward index; 3rd: middle toward ring; 4th: ring away from middle). The 5th finger (little finger) is abducted by abductor digiti minimi. All are innervated by the ulnar nerve (deep branch).

Complete loss of finger abduction with preserved thenar function suggests a deep ulnar nerve palsy at the wrist (e.g., hook of hamate fracture compressing the deep branch in Guyon's canal). This also affects palmar interossei (adduction), hypothenar muscles, and medial two lumbricals.
Q7Application

What is 'ulnar paradox' in the context of ulnar nerve injuries?

Ulnar paradox: A low ulnar nerve lesion (at the wrist) produces MORE visible clawing of 4th and 5th digits than a high lesion (at the elbow).

Explanation: In a high ulnar nerve lesion (at the elbow), the FDP to the 4th and 5th digits is also paralysed (FDP: ulnar nerve-innervated at the elbow). Without FDP, the 4th/5th digits cannot flex at the DIP. The deformity looks less dramatic because both FDP (flexor) and lumbricals (extensor of PIP/DIP) are lost.

In a low lesion (at wrist), FDP is preserved (innervation is proximal). The 4th and 5th digits can flex strongly at DIP (FDP active) but the medial lumbricals are lost — resulting in greater hyperextension at MCP and flexion at PIP/DIP — producing more obvious claw hand.
Q8Application

Describe the safe position for immobilising the hand after injury and explain the anatomical rationale.

The intrinsic plus (POSI — Position of Safe Immobilisation):
• Wrist: 20–30° extension
• MCP joints: 70–90° flexion
• IP joints: full extension
• Thumb: palmar abduction / opposition

Rationale:
• MCP flexion 70–90°: at full flexion, the collateral ligaments (cord and accessory) are at maximum length — immobilising in this position prevents ligamentous shortening (the most common cause of MCP joint stiffness after injury)
• IP extension: prevents IP flexion contracture from shortening of the flexor tendons and palmar plates
• Wrist extension: maintains optimal tenodesis position and prevents volar wrist contracture

Immobilising in MCP extension + IP flexion (the 'intrinsic minus' or fist position) allows collateral ligaments to shorten rapidly and is the main cause of preventable post-trauma hand contracture.
Q9Clinical

Scenario: A 35-year-old office worker presents with tingling in the thumb, index, and middle finger worst at night and during driving. Phalen's test (60 seconds wrist flexion) reproduces symptoms. Tinel's over the carpal tunnel is positive. What are the physiotherapy management options?

Diagnosis: Carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS) — median nerve compression.

Conservative physiotherapy (evidence-based):
• Neutral wrist splinting at night — first-line; prevents nocturnal wrist flexion; reduces intratunnel pressure; evidence level I
• Median nerve mobilisation (neurodynamic exercises / nerve flossing) — improves nerve excursion within the tunnel; evidence supports short-term benefit
• Tendon gliding exercises — maintains flexor tendon glide and prevents adhesion
• Activity modification — avoid sustained wrist flexion/extension, repetitive gripping; ergonomic assessment
• Corticosteroid injection — highly effective for temporary relief; can be combined with physiotherapy
• Surgical referral (carpal tunnel release) if conservative management fails after 3–6 months or in severe CTS (thenar wasting, constant numbness)
Q10Clinical

Scenario: A 52-year-old woman with well-controlled rheumatoid arthritis presents with a 'dropped finger' — she cannot extend her right ring finger at the MCP joint, and the deformity was sudden onset, not painful. What is the likely cause?

Likely cause: Attritional rupture of the extensor digitorum tendon to the ring finger — commonly at the level of the distal ulna where tenosynovitis in RA causes the tendon to fray against prominent ulnar head osteophytes (Vaughan-Jackson syndrome — ruptures progress from ulnar to radial side).

Anatomical basis: RA produces synovitis that erodes the extensor retinaculum and tendon sheaths. The prominent, dorsally subluxed ulnar head in RA (caput ulnae) acts like a saw blade on the overlying extensor tendons during pronation-supination. Sudden loss of active MCP extension without pain is the hallmark — the joint remains passively extendable because the joint capsule and lateral bands are intact.

Management: Surgical tendon repair or transfer (EIP to ring finger EDC) is the primary treatment. Physiotherapy supports post-surgical tendon healing (Kleinert or active place-and-hold protocols for extensor tendon repair), edema management, and splinting.
Q11Clinical

Scenario: A 27-year-old cyclist fractures the hook of hamate after falling onto the handlebar. Three weeks later they report persistent weakness of grip and tingling in the little finger. Explain the anatomical reason for these symptoms.

The hook of hamate projects palmarly from the distal carpal row and forms the medial wall of Guyon's canal. Fracture of the hook:

1. Ulnar nerve compression: The deep branch of the ulnar nerve curves around the hook of hamate at the distal end of Guyon's canal before entering the palm. Fracture fragments can directly compress this nerve → motor weakness of intrinsic hand muscles (interossei, hypothenar muscles, medial lumbricals) and sensory symptoms in the ulnar 1½ digits

2. Flexor tendon rupture risk: The hook creates the medial wall of the carpal tunnel — fractured fragments can lacerate the adjacent flexor tendons (FDP to ring/little finger, FPL) with continued use

Management: Excision of the hook of hamate fragment (primary treatment for athletes); physiotherapy post-excision: ulnar nerve rehabilitation, grip strengthening, progressive return to cycling.
