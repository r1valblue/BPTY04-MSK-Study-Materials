Carpal Tunnel: formed by the concave arch of the carpal bones and covered by the flexor retinaculum. Contains: median nerve + 9 flexor tendons (FDS ×4, FDP ×4, FPL ×1).

Guyon's Canal (Tunnel of Guyon / Ulnar Canal): formed by the pisiform (medially) and hamate hook (laterally) + palmar carpal ligament. Contains the ulnar nerve and artery.

Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex (TFCC)

  • Triangular pad of fibrocartilage acting as the articular disc of the distal radioulnar joint
  • Origin: Sigmoid notch of the radius
  • Insertion: Ulnar styloid
  • Components: articular disc + radioulnar ligaments + ulnocarpal ligaments
Clinical: TFCC tears are a common cause of ulnar-sided wrist pain, especially after a fall on an outstretched hand or rotational injury.

Scaphoid Fracture

  • Early fractures may appear as hairline / non-displaced — no visible fracture line initially
  • Bone resorption that makes fractures visible takes 10–14 days
  • Blood supply is distal-to-proximal (retrograde) → high risk of avascular necrosis if proximal pole is fractured
  • Suspect scaphoid fracture with anatomical snuffbox tenderness after FOOSH injury

Triquetrum Fracture (Often Missed)

  • Fragment is small; overlapping carpal bones can obscure it
  • Pain may be vague or diffuse
  • Focus often incorrectly placed on scaphoid — "if you don't look for the duck, you won't see it"

Lunate Dislocation

  • On a normal lateral wrist X-ray: lunate sits upright, distal articular surface faces slightly forward ("cup holding tea")
  • In lunate dislocation: lunate tips forward (volar tilt) — looks like "a cup that has spilled its contents"
Tip: Always obtain lateral and PA views. Scaphoid waist fractures are better seen in ulnar deviation views. Lunate dislocation is best diagnosed on lateral X-ray.
