1. Overview — Hip versus Shoulder

The hip and shoulder share the same fundamental joint type — a ball-and-socket synovial joint — but are engineered toward opposite ends of the stability–mobility spectrum.

Stability–Mobility Trade-off:
The shoulder prioritises mobility: shallow glenoid, large capsular redundancy, dominant muscular stability (rotator cuff).
The hip prioritises stability: deep acetabulum, acetabular labrum, thick capsule, strong periarticular ligaments — allowing sustained weight bearing across a lifetime.

The acetabulum deepens the socket by roughly 30% via the acetabular labrum — a fibrocartilaginous rim that reduces intraarticular pressure, distributes load, and provides a suction seal. The femoral head is covered entirely by hyaline cartilage (except the fovea capitis), while the acetabular surface is covered only superiorly and posteriorly (the lunate surface), leaving the inferior acetabular notch bridged by the transverse acetabular ligament.

Femoral anteversion (normal ~15°), neck-shaft angle (coxa valga/vara), and acetabular version all affect loading patterns at the hip, knee, and foot throughout the lower limb kinetic chain.

2. Sacroiliac Joint (SIJ)

Slide 3 – SIJ anatomy

The SIJ is a compound joint: the anteroinferior portion is a true synovial joint, while the posterosuperior interosseous surface is a syndesmosis (fibrous). Motion is minimal (~2–4° nutation/counternutation) but clinically significant for load transfer between spine and lower limb.

Nutation and Counternutation

  • Nutation: sacral promontory tips anteriorly relative to ilium — pelvic outlet widens, thoracolumbar fascia and sacrotuberous/sacrospinous ligaments tighten (increases stability)
  • Counternutation: sacral promontory tips posteriorly — increases pelvic inlet, reduces joint compression
Form Closure vs Force Closure:
Form closure = passive stability from joint surface shape and orientation (complementary ridges and grooves of SIJ surfaces, especially after puberty).
Force closure = active/passive stability from ligamentous pre-tension and muscle compression across the joint. Key contributors: transversus abdominis, multifidus, biceps femoris — compressing the SIJ via sacrotuberous ligament tension.

Dysfunction of force closure (e.g., from pregnancy, postpartum ligament laxity, or trunk/hip muscle inhibition) underpins many cases of sacroiliac pain and pelvic girdle pain (PGP).

4. Pubic Symphysis

Slide 8 – Pubic symphysis

Slide 9 – Symphyseal instability

The pubic symphysis is a secondary cartilaginous joint (symphysis) — the two pubic bodies are connected by the interpubic fibrocartilaginous disc. Reinforced by the superior pubic ligament, arcuate (inferior) pubic ligament, and fibres of adductor longus, rectus abdominis, and pyramidalis.

Normally immobile but allows slight springing during gait and significant laxity during pregnancy under relaxin influence. Normal vertical shear is <2 mm; >4 mm suggests instability.

Symphysis Pubis Dysfunction (SPD) / Pelvic Girdle Pain:
Common in pregnancy (hormonal ligament laxity) and following childbirth. Presents as anterior pelvic pain aggravated by weight-bearing asymmetry (stairs, one-leg stance). Management: pelvic girdle belt to augment force closure, activity modification, targeted hip stabiliser strengthening (transversus abdominis, hip abductors/external rotators).

5. Hip Joint

Slide 11 – Hip joint articular surfaces

Slide 12 – Hip joint capsule

Type: ball-and-socket synovial joint. Articulation: femoral head (2/3 sphere) into the acetabulum, whose depth is augmented by the labrum.

Articular Surfaces

  • Femoral head: covered by hyaline cartilage except at the fovea capitis (attachment of ligamentum teres)
  • Acetabulum: lunate surface only covered by hyaline cartilage; central acetabular fossa lined by fat (Haversian fat pad) and synovium
  • Transverse acetabular ligament: bridges the acetabular notch inferiorly, completing the socket and transmitting neurovascular supply via ligamentum teres to femoral head

Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI) & Labral Tears:
CAM impingement: non-spherical femoral head (pistol-grip deformity) jams into acetabular rim in flexion — common in young male athletes.
Pincer impingement: overcoverage of the femoral head by a deep or retroverted acetabulum — compresses labrum at end-range flexion.
Labral tears present with deep groin pain, clicking, and pain on FADIR test. May be managed with arthroscopic labral repair or debridement depending on morphology.

All three extracapsular ligaments spiral around the femoral neck. In extension they tighten (stabilise upright standing with minimal muscle activation). In flexion they unwind — allowing greater ROM and requiring muscular stabilisation.
Total Hip Replacement (THR) Precautions:
Traditional posterior approach precautions avoid hip flexion >90°, adduction past neutral, and internal rotation — positions that load the posterior capsule and risk posterior dislocation. Anterior approach THR may have fewer positional restrictions but requires surgeon-specific protocol.

Iliopsoas: Iliacus and psoas major converge on a common tendon, sharing a bursa (iliopsoas bursa) with the hip joint in ~15% of individuals — communication visible on US in hip OA or inflammatory arthropathy.
Iliopsoas Tendinopathy / Snapping Hip:
"Internal" coxa saltans occurs when the iliopsoas tendon snaps over the iliopectineal eminence or lesser trochanter. Often asymptomatic; if painful, manage with eccentric hip flexor loading and addressing hip capsular restriction in extension.

Trendelenburg Sign & Gait:
When the stance-leg gluteus medius is weak, it cannot stabilise the pelvis against the contralateral limb's body weight. The pelvis drops to the contralateral side (Trendelenburg sign). Compensated Trendelenburg: trunk lurches to the ipsilateral side to shift COM over the stance leg. Common after hip OA, NOF fracture, or gluteal nerve injury.

All six produce hip external rotation. Piriformis and obturator internus also contribute to ABD when the hip is flexed >60° (the ER vector reverses to an ABD vector). They collectively stabilise the femoral head in the acetabulum — analogous to the rotator cuff at the shoulder.

Piriformis Syndrome:
The sciatic nerve typically passes below piriformis (common peroneal above in ~15%). Hypertrophy, spasm, or anatomical variation can compress the sciatic nerve, causing deep gluteal/posterior thigh pain reproduced by the FAIR test (hip flexion, ADD, IR). Distinguish from lumbar radiculopathy by absence of back pain and normal SLR. Treatment: piriformis stretching, deep tissue release, activity modification, image-guided injection in refractory cases.
