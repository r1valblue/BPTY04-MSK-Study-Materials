1. Hip versus Shoulder — Design Principles

Understanding the hip requires grasping one fundamental architectural truth: the hip and shoulder are the same joint type solving radically different engineering problems. Both are ball-and-socket synovial joints, but the shoulder trades stability for mobility, while the hip does the opposite. This is not incidental — it is the direct consequence of their respective mechanical demands.

The shoulder must position the hand in space through enormous arcs of motion, so it accepts the risk of instability and delegates most of its stability to musculature. The hip must transfer the entire superincumbent body weight to the ground through decades of repetitive loading — an average person takes roughly 7,000–9,000 steps per day, and the hip joint reaction force can reach 3–5× bodyweight during normal walking and up to 8× during running. This demands passive structural stability as a baseline, with musculature then fine-tuning and supplementing.

Structural deepening — the labrum's contribution:
The acetabular labrum increases the volume of the acetabulum by approximately 33% and contributes roughly 22% of the total articulating surface. Critically, it creates a negative intra-articular pressure (suction seal) by retaining synovial fluid within the central compartment — this seal actively resists distraction of the femoral head and contributes meaningfully to hip stability. The labrum also functions as a fluid pressurisation system: by slowing fluid extrusion under compressive loads, it maintains a layer of pressurised lubricating fluid over the articular cartilage, reducing wear.

An important nuance: the load the labrum actually transfers varies significantly with hip morphology. In normal hips, the labrum supports only 1–2% of total joint load. In dysplastic hips (where the acetabular coverage is insufficient), the labrum is forced to compensate for the structural deficit and may support 4–11% of total load. This explains why labral pathology and OA accelerate so dramatically in dysplasia — the labrum is being asked to do a job the acetabular bone should be doing.

The femoral neck orientation further shapes hip mechanics. Normal femoral anteversion is ~10–15°; values above this increase the tendency for the hip to lie in IR and can produce intoeing gait with compensatory tibial ER. Neck-shaft angle (normal ~126–128°): coxa valga increases the bending moment through the femoral neck; coxa vara shifts more load onto the medial physis. Understanding this chain of geometry helps explain why seemingly minor structural variants can produce systemic effects through the lower limb.

2. Sacroiliac Joint — Mechanics and Stability

Slide 3 – SIJ anatomy and orientation

The SIJ defies easy classification. Its anteroinferior portion is a true synovial joint with hyaline and fibrocartilaginous articular surfaces. Its posterosuperior portion is a syndesmosis — an interosseous ligamentous mass rather than a conventional articular surface. Motion is minimal (approximately 2–4° of nutation/counternutation and 1–2 mm translation) but biomechanically indispensable for load transfer between the spine and lower limb.

Nutation, Counternutation, and Why They Matter

Nutation occurs when the sacral promontory tips anteroinferiorly relative to the ilium. This is the position adopted during standing and forward bending — it widens the pelvic outlet, brings the ischial tuberosities apart, and critically, tensions the posterior sacroiliac, sacrotuberous, and sacrospinous ligaments. This tensioning is not incidental — it actively compresses the SIJ joint surfaces together, creating passive stability.

Counternutation reverses this: the sacral promontory moves posterosuperiorly. This widens the pelvic inlet (important in labour), reduces posterior ligament tension, and decreases SIJ compression. During the swing phase of gait, one side nutates while the other counternutates — this coupled motion drives ipsilateral hip extension and the rotational momentum of walking.

Form Closure versus Force Closure — Vleeming's model:
Form closure describes the inherent passive stability conferred by the SIJ's bony geometry. Uniquely, the SIJ surfaces are not smooth — they develop complementary ridges and grooves (interlocking irregularities) after puberty that increase friction and resist shear. This is passive, geometry-dependent, and requires no muscular energy. However, perfect form closure would eliminate all motion, so the SIJ surfaces are deliberately imperfect — form closure provides a foundation but is insufficient alone.

Force closure is the active and passive compressive force that makes up the deficit. Key contributors are the posterior oblique sling (gluteus maximus + contralateral latissimus dorsi via thoracolumbar fascia) and the anterior oblique sling (transversus abdominis + internal oblique + contralateral hip adductors). The sacrotuberous ligament stores and releases elastic energy through its connection to biceps femoris — hip extension during gait loads the lig, increasing SIJ compression at exactly the moment maximal stability is needed.

This model has direct clinical consequences. Conditions that impair force closure — pregnancy (relaxin-mediated ligament laxity), post-partum deconditioning, lumbar surgery, inhibition of transversus abdominis — can produce pelvic instability even when the bony architecture is intact. The physiotherapist's role is not to "fix the SIJ" but to restore the neuromuscular force closure system that keeps it stable.

Clinical Assessment of the SIJ

No single provocative test reliably identifies SIJ pathology. The current best evidence supports using a cluster of tests: the Active Straight Leg Raise (ASLR) (positive if load transfer difficulty is reported), Posterior Pelvic Pain Provocation (P4/thigh thrust), and compression/distraction tests. Three or more positive tests from a validated cluster substantially increases diagnostic accuracy. The ASLR is particularly useful because it directly tests force closure — if the patient can complete it more easily with manual compression of the iliac wings (simulating a pelvic belt), the diagnosis of SIJ force closure insufficiency is strongly supported.

The pelvic ligaments are among the strongest in the body, and for good reason: they must resist enormous shear and compression forces while simultaneously permitting the small but essential motions of the SIJ. Rather than thinking of them as independent structures, consider them as an interconnected tensioning system — each ligament pre-tensions others through coupled anatomy.

NAVY mnemonic — greater sciatic foramen contents (lateral to medial):
Nerve (sciatic + superior/inferior gluteal nerves) · Artery (superior and inferior gluteal) · Vein (superior and inferior gluteal) · Yet another nerve (posterior cutaneous nerve of thigh, nerve to piriformis, nerve to quadratus femoris)

The piriformis exits the greater sciatic foramen and divides it into a suprapiriform and infrapiriform compartment. Most structures — including the sciatic nerve — pass through the infrapiriform compartment. Sciatic nerve variants (passing through or above piriformis) are the anatomical basis for piriformis syndrome.

The lesser sciatic foramen, formed by the lesser sciatic notch plus the sacrotuberous and sacrospinous ligaments, transmits the obturator internus tendon (as it exits the pelvis to reach the greater trochanter), the pudendal nerve, and the internal pudendal vessels.

4. Pubic Symphysis and Pelvic Girdle Pain

Slide 8 – Pubic symphysis anatomy

Slide 9 – Symphyseal instability and PGP

The pubic symphysis is a secondary cartilaginous joint (amphiarthrosis), connecting the two pubic bodies via an interpubic fibrocartilaginous disc. It is reinforced superiorly by the superior pubic ligament, inferiorly by the arcuate (inferior) pubic ligament (the thickest), and dynamically by the tendinous expansions of rectus abdominis, adductor longus, and gracilis. This confluence of tendinous forces makes the symphysis pubis the convergence point of the entire anterior abdominal and thigh musculature — a fact with significant implications for athletic groin pain.

In health, the symphysis allows only minimal motion — a slight spring of 1–2 mm of vertical shear during gait. During pregnancy, the hormone relaxin dramatically increases ligamentous laxity across the entire pelvis, and symphyseal width can increase from a normal ~5 mm to 9 mm or more. Vertical instability exceeding 4 mm suggests pathological disruption.

Pelvic Girdle Pain (PGP) and Symphysis Pubis Dysfunction (SPD):
PGP is an umbrella term encompassing pain at the SIJ and/or pubic symphysis, most commonly seen in pregnancy (affecting 20–25% of pregnant women to varying degrees). SPD specifically refers to symptomatic symphyseal instability or widening.

The mechanism is a failure of force closure — relaxin softens ligaments beyond the capacity of the musculature to compensate, leaving the pelvic ring dynamically unstable. This manifests as pain with weight-bearing asymmetry: walking up stairs, single-leg stance, and rolling over in bed are characteristically provocative because each requires one-sided loading that a lax pelvis cannot buffer.

Assessment: Positive ASLR, pain on posterior pelvic pain provocation test (P4/thigh thrust), pain on FABER. The "symphysis click" is a specific but insensitive sign.
Management: Pelvic compression belt (augments passive force closure), symmetrical activity modification (avoid wide-leg stepping, reduce one-leg stance tasks), targeted transversus abdominis + hip abductor/ER strengthening to restore active force closure. Delivery usually resolves symptoms, but recovery may take months.

Outside pregnancy, the symphysis is relevant in athletic pubalgia / sports hernia — chronic overload of the rectus abdominis and adductor longus attachments at the pubic crest, seen in sports requiring rapid direction changes (football, ice hockey, soccer). This condition is distinct from an inguinal hernia and from adductor strains, but anatomically they may all co-exist.

5. Hip Joint — Articular Mechanics

Slide 11 – Hip joint articular surfaces

Slide 12 – Hip capsule orientation

The hip is a ball-and-socket synovial joint — but this classification undersells its sophistication. The femoral head is approximately 2/3 of a sphere, with a radius matching that of the acetabular lunate surface almost precisely. The close congruency distributes load over a large contact area, protecting the cartilage from focal stress concentrations. However, this congruency is imperfect by design: a perfectly spherical head in a perfectly hemispherical socket would have no degrees of freedom in rotation — the complementary surfaces would lock together. The slight geometric mismatches allow the multiaxial rotation the hip requires while maintaining near-maximal contact area through a range of positions.

Articular Cartilage Distribution and Load Paths

The femoral head is covered by hyaline cartilage everywhere except the fovea capitis, where the ligamentum teres inserts. Cartilage thickness is greatest superolaterally — exactly where peak loads are transmitted during single-leg stance and mid-gait. The acetabular articular surface is the lunate surface (horseshoe-shaped), covering the superior and posteroinferior walls but leaving the central acetabular fossa exposed. This fossa contains the Haversian fat pad (a cushioning structure) and synovium, and is bridged inferiorly by the transverse acetabular ligament.

Load passes primarily through the superior dome of the acetabulum into the posterior column — the biomechanically robust arc running from the ischial tuberosity through the posterior wall to the ilium. This explains the characteristic pattern of OA cartilage loss in the superolateral zone, and why acetabular dysplasia (reduced coverage) accelerates cartilage failure — the same load is concentrated over a smaller surface area.

Femoroacetabular Impingement (FAI) — Pathomechanics and Management:
FAI syndrome (FAIS) represents a motion-related disorder where altered joint morphology causes pain and damage at end-range, particularly in flexion. Two morphological subtypes exist, though most patients have mixed morphology:

CAM morphology: a non-spherical femoral head with excess bone at the anterosuperior head-neck junction (increased alpha angle, typically >55°). During hip flexion the cam lesion jams into the anterosuperior acetabular rim, shearing the labrum and cartilage. This is the more common and more destructive form — it is the labrum and cartilage that are damaged, not the bony prominences themselves.

Pincer morphology: overcoverage of the femoral head by a deep (coxa profunda) or retroverted acetabulum. The femoral neck impinges against the acetabular rim earlier in flexion, pinching the labrum between femur and acetabulum. Over time the opposite posterior cartilage can develop a "contrecoup" lesion from levering of the femoral head out of the socket.

Clinical presentation: anterior groin pain, especially in flexion-loaded positions (sitting, squats); positive FADIR (sensitivity ~60%, specificity ~18% — useful screening rule-in, poor rule-out); clicking or catching sensation.
Physiotherapy: Current evidence supports supervised physiotherapy with activity modification, patient education on pathomechanics, strength training targeting hip stabilisers, and motor control retraining to reduce impingement positions. Outcomes comparable to early surgical intervention for mild-moderate symptoms. Surgery (arthroscopic osteoplasty ± labral repair) reserved for failed conservative management.

6. Hip Capsule and Ligamentous Restraints

Slide 13 – Intracapsular ligaments (anterior view)

Slide 14 – Extracapsular capsular ligaments

The hip capsule is a thick fibrous sleeve enclosing the femoral neck entirely. Its fibres run in two directions: longitudinal fibres run neck-to-pelvis, while circular fibres (the zona orbicularis) wrap around the femoral neck, acting as a collar that resists distraction of the femoral head. The three extracapsular reinforcing ligaments — iliofemoral, pubofemoral, and ischiofemoral — spiral around the capsule and tighten in extension, making the extended hip extremely stable with minimal muscular input.

Ligamentum teres Transverse acetabular lig + acetabular notch → fovea capitis Adduction in flexion (variable) Transmits foveal artery (branch of obturator a.) — the primary blood supply to the femoral head in children, contributing less in adults (where the lateral epiphyseal arteries via retinacular vessels dominate).
The spiral capsule and energy efficiency:
The spiralling arrangement of the three capsular ligaments is one of the most elegant designs in the body. In full extension, all three ligaments wind tight simultaneously, producing a passive "close-packed" position that requires virtually no muscular energy to maintain — this is how humans can stand comfortably for extended periods without significant quadriceps or hip extensor activity. In flexion, the ligaments unwind, releasing ROM but requiring muscular substitution for stability.
THR Precautions — anatomical rationale:
In a posterior approach total hip replacement, the posterior capsule and short external rotators (piriformis, gemelli, OI, QF) are divided to gain surgical access. Until the posterior capsule heals and the deep rotators are rehabilitated, the hip is vulnerable to posterior dislocation — the direction of least resistance. The classic precautions (no flexion >90°, no ADD past neutral, no IR) directly avoid the positions that would leverage the femoral head posteriorly out of the socket. Anterior approach THR preserves the posterior structures, so traditional posterior precautions do not apply — patients should follow their surgeon's specific protocol.

Iliopsoas as a unit — and its clinical implications:
Despite separate origins, iliacus and psoas major share a common tendon that passes under the inguinal ligament through the muscular lacuna and over the anterior hip capsule. An iliopsoas bursa — the largest bursa in the body — lies between the tendon and capsule, and communicates with the hip joint in ~15% of individuals. In hip OA or inflammatory arthritis, joint effusion can extend into this bursa, appearing as an anterior hip mass or causing anterior groin pain that mimics a hernia.

Psoas tightness has a distinct clinical pattern: the patient's hip lies in flexion when supine (Thomas test positive), and the lumbar lordosis increases in sitting to compensate. In patients with chronic low back pain, differentiating psoas tightness from disc pathology is clinically important.
Internal Snapping Hip (Coxa Saltans Interna) and Iliopsoas Tendinopathy:
The iliopsoas tendon can snap over the iliopectineal eminence or the lesser trochanter, producing an audible or palpable snap during hip flexion-extension — particularly as the tendon moves from lateral (in flexion) to medial (in extension) relative to the iliopectineal eminence. This is coxa saltans interna.

Often asymptomatic and requiring no intervention, but when painful (typically in dancers and gymnasts from repetitive loading), it presents as anterior hip pain with resisted flexion or passive extension. Ultrasound can visualise the tendon snap in real time. Management: eccentric hip flexor strengthening, restoration of hip extension ROM (often limited by capsular restriction), and avoidance of provocative positions during load. Corticosteroid injection into the bursa can reduce pain acutely but does not address the mechanical cause.

Gluteus medius — the real story of Trendelenburg:
The Trendelenburg sign is seen in textbooks as simply "gluteus medius weakness = pelvis drops." The reality is more nuanced and more important.

Gluteus medius has two functional roles during stance: (1) it prevents contralateral pelvic drop (frontal plane stability via the hip abduction moment), and (2) its posterior portion contributes to ER and extension, helping control femoral IR during loading response. Current evidence shows that hip abductor strength alone does not consistently predict pelvic drop magnitude — neuromuscular timing and recruitment pattern matter as much as raw strength.

Compensated Trendelenburg (ipsilateral trunk lean) is a strategy to shift the centre of mass over the stance limb, reducing the demand on the abductors. It is energy-efficient in the short term but increases the ipsilateral knee valgus moment and lateral lumbar compression — a meaningful risk factor for knee and back pathology over time.

Clinical implication: don't just strengthen the hip abductors. Target posterior gluteus medius (resisted ER + extension), train in single-leg stance conditions at progressively higher speeds and loads, and address trunk motor control.

The Six Deep External Rotators

The six deep hip external rotators (piriformis, obturator internus and externus, superior and inferior gemelli, quadratus femoris) collectively perform a function at the hip directly analogous to the rotator cuff at the shoulder — they compress the femoral head into the acetabulum and provide rotational fine-tuning across the joint. Like the rotator cuff, they are often inhibited before the prime movers in pathological conditions.

Piriformis Syndrome — an underdiagnosed cause of gluteal and sciatic pain:
The sciatic nerve classically passes through the infrapiriform compartment of the greater sciatic foramen, below piriformis. However, in approximately 15–20% of people there is anatomical variation — most commonly the common peroneal branch passing through or above piriformis (the "normal" tibial branch passes below). This explains why the condition exists: in susceptible anatomies, piriformis hypertrophy, spasm, or scar tissue can compress the sciatic nerve or its divisions.

Clinical picture: deep, aching gluteal pain (not the familiar "electricity down the back of the leg" quality of true sciatica) that worsens with prolonged sitting and is reproduced by the FAIR test (hip Flexion, Adduction, and Internal Rotation with the patient sidelying). Pain typically does not change with lumbar movements (distinguishing it from L4–L5 or L5–S1 radiculopathy). SLR is usually negative.

Imaging: MRI pelvis can reveal piriformis asymmetry. Nerve conduction studies occasionally show peroneal division slowing. Treatment: piriformis stretching (in FAIR position), deep tissue release, activity modification, and in refractory cases ultrasound-guided corticosteroid or botulinum toxin injection. Addressing hip kinematic drivers (limited hip ER or IR) is essential for long-term management.
