Q1Recall

Name the four joints of the shoulder complex and classify each by type.

• Sternoclavicular (SC) joint — Saddle joint; only true skeletal connection between upper limb and axial skeleton
• Acromioclavicular (AC) joint — Plane joint; 3 degrees of freedom
• Scapulothoracic (ST) joint — Not a true synovial joint; functional articulation between scapula and thoracic wall
• Glenohumeral (GH) joint — Ball and socket (spheroidal); most mobile joint in the body
Q2Recall

Name the four rotator cuff muscles, their origin, insertion, and primary action.

• Supraspinatus: supraspinous fossa → greater tubercle (superior facet) → initiation of abduction (0–15°); depression of humeral head
• Infraspinatus: infraspinous fossa → greater tubercle (middle facet) → external rotation; humeral head depression
• Teres minor: lateral border scapula → greater tubercle (inferior facet) → external rotation; humeral head depression
• Subscapularis: subscapular fossa → lesser tubercle → internal rotation; humeral head depression

Mnemonic: SITS
Q3Recall

What is the scapulohumeral rhythm and what does a disruption indicate?

During shoulder abduction, the glenohumeral and scapulothoracic joints move in a coordinated ratio of 2:1 (GH:ST) — for every 3° of shoulder abduction, 2° occurs at GH and 1° at scapulothoracic (scapular upward rotation).

Total 180° shoulder abduction = ~120° GH + ~60° scapular upward rotation.

Disruption (scapular dyskinesis): altered scapular motion reduces the subacromial space, increases impingement risk, alters rotator cuff length-tension, and reduces force transmission. Assessed clinically by observing scapular position and movement during arm elevation.
Q4Recall

What are the glenohumeral ligaments (GHL)? Name and describe all three.

• Superior GHL: superior glenoid → lesser tubercle; resists inferior translation and external rotation
• Middle GHL: anterior glenoid → anatomical neck; resists anterior translation at 45° abduction
• Inferior GHL (IGHL): inferior glenoid rim → surgical neck; the primary stabiliser at 90° abduction + external rotation (the position of dislocation)

The IGHL is the most important of the three and forms the "hammock" supporting the humeral head. It is the structure most commonly damaged in anterior GH dislocation.
Q5Recall

What muscles produce scapular upward rotation and why is this important for shoulder function?

Scapular upward rotation is produced by the force couple of:
• Upper trapezius (pulls superior angle up and laterally)
• Lower trapezius (pulls inferior angle medially)
• Serratus anterior (pulls inferior angle laterally and anteriorly around the chest wall)

This is critical because: (1) it directs the glenoid fossa superiorly to face the elevating humerus, maintaining congruency; (2) it prevents subacromial impingement by maintaining or increasing the subacromial space; (3) it positions the deltoid-rotator cuff force couple optimally. Serratus anterior paralysis (long thoracic nerve injury) causes winging of the scapula and loss of effective arm elevation.
Q6Application

Explain the 'force couple' mechanism that allows the deltoid to abduct the shoulder despite its line of pull creating superior shear on the humeral head.

The deltoid's superior fibres pull the humerus directly upward — alone, this would impinge the greater tubercle under the acromion and produce superior subluxation. The rotator cuff (particularly subscapularis anteriorly and infraspinatus/teres minor posteriorly) acts as a depressor — their line of pull compresses and depresses the humeral head. Together, deltoid and rotator cuff form a force couple: deltoid creates the abduction moment, rotator cuff counteracts the shear and keeps the humeral head centred. Without a functioning rotator cuff (e.g., massive tear), deltoid action produces superior head migration and loss of effective abduction.
Q7Application

A patient has a positive Neer's impingement test (passive forward flexion produces superior shoulder pain at ~80–120°). What anatomical structure is being compressed and between what?

Neer's sign: Passive forward flexion (with the arm medially rotated) reproduces pain when the greater tubercle (with attached supraspinatus/subacromial bursa) is forced against the anterior edge of the acromion and the coracoacromial ligament.

The structure compressed is most commonly the supraspinatus tendon and/or the subacromial bursa, caught between the greater tubercle and the coracoacromial arch. Structural factors contributing to narrowing include:
• Hooked (Type III) acromion
• Thickened coracoacromial ligament
• Subacromial bursal thickening
• Loss of scapular upward rotation (reducing subacromial space)
• Decreased superior GH joint glide (posteroinferior capsule tightness)
Q8Application

Why is the glenohumeral joint the most commonly dislocated joint in the body? What structural factors contribute?

The GH joint prioritises mobility over stability. Structural factors predisposing to dislocation:
• Shallow glenoid fossa — only covers ~25–30% of the humeral head surface (a golf ball on a tee analogy)
• Large humeral head relative to glenoid
• Loose articular capsule — allows enormous ROM
• No bony restraint anteroinferiorly — where 95% of dislocations occur

Stability relies heavily on dynamic factors (rotator cuff), the labrum (+deepening), the GHLs, negative intra-articular pressure, and adhesion-cohesion of synovial fluid. Anterior dislocations occur in ABER (abduction, external rotation, extension). The IGHL and anteroinferior labrum (Bankart lesion) are typically injured.
Q9Clinical

Scenario: A 25-year-old rugby player dislocates his right shoulder anteriorly for the third time. He has a positive apprehension test at 90° abduction / external rotation. MRI shows a Bankart lesion and a large Hill-Sachs defect. Outline the anatomy of these lesions.

• Bankart lesion: Avulsion of the anteroinferior glenoid labrum (and often the IGHL attachment) from the glenoid rim. The labrum provides approximately 22% of glenoid depth; its loss significantly reduces static stability. A bony Bankart involves fracture of the anterior glenoid rim itself.

• Hill-Sachs defect: Compression fracture (impaction) of the posterosuperior humeral head, created when the humeral head drives into the anterior glenoid rim at the moment of dislocation. Large Hill-Sachs defects can engage with the anterior glenoid during external rotation ('engaging' Hill-Sachs), mechanically blocking reduction and requiring surgical consideration (Latarjet procedure or remplissage).

Physiotherapy role: Conservative management after first-time anterior dislocation (especially in older patients); post-surgical rehabilitation (Latarjet: 12+ weeks return to contact sport) focusing on rotator cuff and periscapular strengthening, proprioception, and graduated sport-specific loading.
Q10Clinical

Scenario: A 68-year-old woman has been unable to actively abduct her shoulder beyond 20° for 6 months following a minor fall. MRI shows a complete supraspinatus tear and a small infraspinatus tear. She asks whether surgery is necessary.

Evidence-based answer: For symptomatic rotator cuff tears in older patients, high-quality evidence supports conservative management as the first-line approach.

Key points:
• Many full-thickness tears (including complete supraspinatus) are asymptomatic — incidental finding rate increases with age (>50% of people over 70 have tears)
• RCT evidence (Kukkonen et al., 2015; Moosmayer et al., 2019) shows physiotherapy-based rehabilitation achieves equivalent outcomes to surgical repair for non-traumatic tears in patients over 55–60
• Physiotherapy: periscapular strengthening (trapezius, serratus anterior to optimise scapulohumeral rhythm); intact rotator cuff strengthening (infraspinatus, subscapularis force couple); deltoid strengthening; posterior capsule stretching; proprioception
• Surgical referral should be considered if: trauma in a young patient, rapid onset of complete loss of function, failure of 3–6 months of structured physiotherapy
Q11Clinical

Scenario: A patient is post-op day 3 following a Bankart repair (labral re-attachment). Their surgical protocol says 'avoid ER beyond neutral for 6 weeks'. What anatomical structure are you protecting and why?

You are protecting the repaired anteroinferior labrum and the IGHL complex.

The Bankart repair re-attaches the torn labrum to the anterior glenoid rim (via suture anchors). The IGHL attaches to the anteroinferior labrum — together they resist anterior translation at 90° ABER. External rotation at 90° abduction places the maximum tensile stress on the anteroinferior capsulolabral complex. Allowing full external rotation before the repair has healed (~6 weeks for initial ligamentous healing) risks re-tearing the suture anchor fixation or re-avulsing the labrum.

The 6-week restriction allows type III collagen (scar tissue) formation to bridge the repair; subsequent physiotherapy gradually restores external rotation with progressive loading once the repair is mechanically sufficient.
Q12Clinical

Scenario: A 55-year-old presents with a painful, severely restricted shoulder — passive ROM is reduced in all planes (ER limited to 10°, abduction to 80°, flexion to 100°). She reports gradual onset over 6 months. What is the likely diagnosis and what is the physiotherapy approach?

Diagnosis: Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder) — characterised by global restriction in a capsular pattern (ER > ABD > IR most restricted) with pain at end range.

Anatomical basis: Inflammation and fibrosis of the GH joint capsule — particularly the anteroinferior pouch and coracohumeral ligament. The capsule thickens and contracts, obliterating the axillary fold (normally capacious). Volume of the joint cavity reduces from ~28 mL to <10 mL.

Physiotherapy approach (evidence-based):
• Pain management phase: gentle pendular exercises, pain-free ROM, heat before exercise
• Progressive mobilisation: grade III-IV joint mobilisations targeting inferior and anterior GH glides; stretching of the posteroinferior capsule
• Home programme: progressive capsular stretching, particularly ER and elevation
• Corticosteroid injection: highly effective in the painful/freezing phase (refer for injection before intensive manual therapy)
• Realistic timeline: natural history 1–3 years; physiotherapy accelerates recovery but does not always change final outcome
