Section 1

Overview of the Shoulder Girdle

The shoulder girdle is not a single joint but a complex of four articulations working in precise coordination to produce the widest range of motion of any joint in the body. This mobility, however, comes at a significant cost to stability — the glenohumeral joint relies almost entirely on soft-tissue structures (labrum, capsule, ligaments, and above all the rotator cuff musculature) rather than bony congruence for integrity.

📌 Key Concept The shoulder girdle is composed of three bones (clavicle, scapula, humerus) and four functional articulations: the sternoclavicular (SC) joint, the acromioclavicular (AC) joint, the glenohumeral (GH) joint, and the scapulothoracic (ST) interface. Full shoulder movement requires all four to work simultaneously — restriction at any one directly limits the others.

Understanding the shoulder requires grasping one central anatomical principle: the glenoid fossa is extremely shallow. The humeral head is roughly four times larger than the glenoid surface area, so only about 25–30% of it contacts the glenoid at any one time. This is what allows such extraordinary mobility but also explains the frequency of shoulder pathology compared to, say, the hip.

Section 2

Key Osteology

The shoulder girdle osteology is covered in detail in the Upper Limb Osteology topic. The points below focus specifically on features relevant to understanding joint mechanics and muscle attachments in this module.

Clavicle

The clavicle is the only bony connection between the upper limb and the axial skeleton. It acts as a strut, holding the shoulder laterally away from the thorax and transmitting compressive forces from the arm to the sternum. Its S-shaped curvature gives it resilience. The lateral third of the clavicle is the origin of the upper trapezius and anterior deltoid, and the insertion of the costoclavicular ligament keeps its medial end anchored to the first rib.

🧠 Why This Matters The clavicle is the most commonly fractured bone in the body (particularly at the junction of its medial two-thirds and lateral third — the weakest point). A clavicle fracture can disrupt the entire shoulder girdle mechanism because the strut function is lost, causing the shoulder to drop medially and inferiorly.

Scapula

The scapula is a flat, triangular bone that sits on the posterolateral thorax between T2–T7. Its mobility over the thoracic wall (at the scapulothoracic interface) is essential for overhead arm movement. Key landmarks to know for this topic:

  • Supraspinous fossa — origin of supraspinatus
  • Infraspinous fossa — origin of infraspinatus
  • Subscapular fossa (costal surface) — origin of subscapularis
  • Lateral border — origin of teres minor (superior 2/3) and teres major (inferior angle)
  • Medial border — insertion of serratus anterior (anterior surface) and rhomboids (posterior surface)
  • Coracoid process — origin of coracobrachialis and pectoralis minor; attachment of coracoclavicular ligament
  • Glenoid fossa + glenoid labrum — articulates with humeral head; labrum deepens the fossa by ~50%
  • Acromion — forms the AC joint with clavicle; forms the subacromial space above the supraspinatus

Humerus

The head of the humerus faces medially, superiorly, and posteriorly (retroversion ~30°). Key landmarks:

  • Greater tubercle — insertion of supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor (SITS except S)
  • Lesser tubercle — insertion of subscapularis
  • Intertubercular (bicipital) groove — long head of biceps travels here; medial lip = insertion of teres major and latissimus dorsi; lateral lip = insertion of pectoralis major
  • Deltoid tuberosity — insertion of deltoid (all three parts)
  • Anatomical neck — site of glenohumeral ligament attachment
  • Surgical neck — most common fracture site; axillary nerve and posterior circumflex humeral artery are at risk
Section 3

Sternoclavicular (SC) Joint

The SC joint is the only synovial articulation between the upper limb and the axial skeleton. Despite its small size, it is mechanically crucial: every movement of the shoulder girdle — elevation, depression, protraction, retraction — involves motion here.

Classification and Structure

The SC joint is classified as a saddle-type synovial joint (biaxial), though in practice it behaves as a multiaxial joint because of an intra-articular fibrocartilaginous articular disc that divides the joint into two compartments. This disc is vital — it compensates for the incongruence between the sternal end of the clavicle and the clavicular notch of the manubrium, allowing a third axis of movement (rotation) that would otherwise be impossible.

Articular surfaces are covered in fibrocartilage rather than hyaline cartilage — an unusual feature that reflects the compressive and shear forces transmitted through this joint from arm loading.

📌 Key Concept — The Articular Disc The articular disc absorbs forces so effectively that dislocation of the SC joint is rare — it takes enormous force (e.g., direct impact or compressive load transmitted from the arm). When it does occur, a posterior dislocation is the more dangerous variant, as the posterior capsule is weaker and retrosternal structures (trachea, great vessels) can be compromised.

Movements

The SC joint permits: elevation and depression of the clavicle (shrugging), protraction and retraction of the shoulder, and passive axial rotation of the clavicle (occurs when the arm is raised above 90° as the scapula rotates upward).

🏥 Clinical Relevance — SC Joint Dislocation Anterior dislocation (more common): blow to the anterior shoulder rotating it backwards. Visible prominence at the SC joint. Posterior dislocation: direct blow or force driving shoulder forward. May compress the trachea, oesophagus, or great vessels — a surgical emergency. In adolescents the epiphyseal growth plate at the sternal end of the clavicle does not fully close until age 22–25, so "dislocation" in younger patients is often a physeal fracture.
Section 4

Acromioclavicular (AC) Joint

The AC joint is a plane (gliding) synovial joint formed between the lateral end of the clavicle and the acromion of the scapula. It is described as a single-plane joint but actually has three degrees of freedom, allowing the scapula to move relative to the clavicle during arm elevation and rotation.

Movements at the AC Joint

  • Protraction–Retraction of the scapula
  • Elevation–Depression (total ROM ~15°)
  • Medial–Lateral Rotation of the scapula (total ROM ~30°)

Ligaments

LigamentPrimary Function
Acromioclavicular ligamentReinforces the articular capsule; stabilises the AC joint in all directions. Primary restraint to posterior translation and axial rotation.
Coracoclavicular lig. — Trapezoid (anterior)Prevents anterior dislocation and medial override of the clavicle onto the acromion
Coracoclavicular lig. — Conoid (posterior)Prevents upward displacement of the clavicle relative to the coracoid; main vertical stabiliser
Coracoacromial ligamentForms the coracoacromial arch with the acromion and coracoid. Not a joint ligament per se — prevents superior escape of the humeral head from the GH joint.
🧠 Mnemonic — Coracoclavicular Ligaments "Trap before Con" — Trapezoid is anterior, Conoid is posterior. Together they form the coracoclavicular ligament, which is the primary vertical stabiliser of the AC joint. If both are torn, the clavicle rises relative to the acromion (visible step deformity).
🏥 Clinical Relevance — AC Joint Sprain / Separation AC joint injuries are graded I–VI (Rockwood classification). A direct fall onto the point of the shoulder (olecranon or acromion) is the typical mechanism. Grade I: AC ligament sprain only. Grade II: AC ligament torn, coracoclavicular intact. Grade III: Both AC and coracoclavicular ligaments torn — visible step deformity ("piano key" sign). Grades IV–VI involve clavicle displacement through the trapezius, beneath the coracoid, or inferiorly — all require surgical fixation.
Section 5

Glenohumeral (GH) Joint

The GH joint is a spheroidal (ball-and-socket) synovial joint — the most mobile joint in the human body. It achieves this by sacrificing bony constraint: the humeral head (large sphere) articulates with the glenoid fossa (a shallow oval socket), providing inherent instability that must be managed by the surrounding soft tissues at all times.

Why is the GH Joint So Unstable?

🧠 Anatomical Reasoning The radius of curvature of the humeral head is significantly larger than that of the glenoid — the joint surfaces are incongruent. Only ~25% of the humeral head contacts the glenoid at any point. Additionally, gravity constantly acts to pull the humerus inferiorly out of the socket. This means the GH joint requires active muscular control (principally the rotator cuff) and passive soft-tissue constraint (labrum, capsule, ligaments) simultaneously for stability.

Deepening Mechanisms — The Glenoid Labrum

The glenoid labrum is a fibrocartilaginous ring attached around the periphery of the glenoid. It deepens the socket by approximately 50%, increasing the contact area between the humeral head and glenoid. It also provides an attachment point for the glenohumeral ligaments and the long head of biceps brachii (at the supraglenoid tubercle). Labral tears (e.g., SLAP lesions — Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior) are a major source of shoulder instability and pain.

Capsule and Ligaments

The joint capsule is loose inferiorly (forming the axillary recess) to accommodate full abduction. The transverse humeral ligament runs between the greater and lesser tubercles, retaining the long head of biceps in the intertubercular groove.

Bursae

Several bursae reduce friction around the GH joint. The most clinically important is the subacromial bursa (between the acromion/coracoacromial arch and the supraspinatus tendon). Inflammation of this bursa (subacromial bursitis) is one of the most common causes of shoulder pain and is tightly linked with supraspinatus tendon impingement.

📌 The Abduction Arc — A Key Concept Full shoulder abduction to 180° is not achieved by the GH joint alone. It requires the scapulohumeral rhythm: for every 2° of GH abduction, 1° of scapular upward rotation occurs (2:1 ratio). This means ~120° comes from GH abduction and ~60° from scapular upward rotation (driven by upper and lower trapezius + serratus anterior). Loss of scapular control ("winging") dramatically limits this range.
🏥 Clinical Relevance — GH Dislocation GH dislocation is by far the most common large joint dislocation in the body. Anterior dislocation (95%+) occurs with the arm in abduction and external rotation (e.g., throwing, falling on outstretched hand). The humeral head displaces anteroinferiorly through the inferior capsule. The axillary nerve (winds around the surgical neck) and the long head of biceps tendon are at risk. The Hill-Sachs lesion (compression fracture of the posterolateral humeral head) and Bankart lesion (anterior glenoid labral tear) are classic associated injuries.
Section 6

Scapulothoracic (ST) Mechanism

The scapulothoracic articulation is not a true synovial joint — there is no cartilage, capsule, or synovial membrane. It is a physiological joint: the subscapularis muscle glides against the serratus anterior muscle on the posterior thoracic wall, and movement is possible wherever these muscles are present. The scapula can therefore glide freely across ribs 2–7.

Despite its absence of formal joint structures, the ST mechanism is arguably the most important for overall shoulder function. The muscles that act here (trapezius, serratus anterior, rhomboids, levator scapulae) position and stabilise the glenoid fossa in the optimal orientation for GH joint movement. Weakness or paralysis of any of these muscles causes "winging" of the scapula — the medial border lifts away from the thorax, collapsing the scapular base and reducing GH abduction power.

🏥 Clinical Relevance — Serratus Anterior Palsy The long thoracic nerve (C5–C7) has a long, superficial course and is vulnerable to traction injury, surgery, or repetitive overhead strain. Paralysis of serratus anterior abolishes scapular protraction and upward rotation, causing medial winging (medial border lifts posteriorly). The patient cannot abduct the arm above 90° because the glenoid cannot be upwardly rotated to allow full elevation.
Section 7

Muscles of the Shoulder Girdle (Extrinsic)

Extrinsic muscles originate from the axial skeleton (skull, vertebrae, ribs) and insert onto the scapula or clavicle. Their primary role is scapular positioning: they set the platform on which GH movement occurs. They fall into two functional groups — muscles that move or stabilise the scapula and muscles that reinforce the clavicle.

Trapezius — Three Functional Parts

The trapezius covers the entire posterior neck and upper back. It is innervated by the spinal accessory nerve (CN XI) with proprioceptive input from C3–C4. Its three parts act differently, which makes it a uniquely versatile muscle:

PartOriginInsertionAction
Upper (descending) Occipital bone, nuchal ligament, C7 spinous process Lateral 1/3 of clavicle Elevates scapula; ipsilateral neck inclination (unilateral); neck extension (bilateral)
Middle (transverse) Spinous processes T1–T4 Acromion Retracts (adducts) the scapula
Lower (ascending) Spinous processes T5–T12 Spine of scapula Retracts + depresses the scapula; upward rotation of glenoid
📌 The Trapezius Force Couple Upper and lower trapezius work together with serratus anterior as a force couple to rotate the scapula upward during arm elevation. Upper trap elevates the clavicle (via the AC joint chain), lower trap pulls the scapular spine inferiorly and medially, and serratus pulls the inferior angle anterolaterally — together creating a rotation moment around the scapula. Weakness in any component disrupts scapular kinematics.
🏥 Clinical — Testing the Accessory Nerve The most common cause of accessory nerve (CN XI) damage is iatrogenic — cervical lymph node biopsy or internal jugular vein cannulation can injure the nerve as it passes through the posterior triangle of the neck. Damage causes trapezius weakness: ask the patient to shrug their shoulders against resistance. Look also for asymmetric neckline, dropped shoulder, and difficulty with arm elevation above 90°.

🧠 Mnemonic — Scapular Rotators Upward rotation (glenoid faces up, needed for arm elevation): Serratus anterior + Upper and Lower trapezius (SUL).
Downward rotation (glenoid faces down): Rhomboids + Levator scapulae + Pectoralis minor (RLP).
These act as antagonistic pairs — weakness in one group causes the scapula to sit in the other group's preferred position at rest.
Section 8

Rotator Cuff (SITS)

The rotator cuff comprises four intrinsic scapulohumeral muscles that originate from the scapula and insert onto the proximal humerus. Their collective resting tone keeps the humeral head centred in the glenoid fossa — a dynamic compression mechanism that is the primary source of GH joint stability. Without the rotator cuff, the deltoid's superior pull would simply dislocate the humeral head upward into the subacromial space with every attempt at abduction.

🧠 Mnemonic — SITS Supraspinatus · Infraspinatus · Teres minor · Subscapularis
Remember that three insert onto the greater tubercle (SIT — Supra, Infra, Teres minor) and one inserts onto the lesser tubercle (Subscapularis — the "S" at the end, which also acts oppositely to the others, producing internal rather than external rotation).

📌 The Abduction Paradox — Supraspinatus vs Deltoid The supraspinatus is often described as the initiator of abduction (0–15°), but this is somewhat oversimplified. The true role of the supraspinatus is humeral head compression into the glenoid, preventing the deltoid from simply translating the humerus upward. Without supraspinatus, the deltoid still abducts the arm — but with a painful arc as the torn tendon/bursa impinges under the acromion. The clinical test for supraspinatus is the empty can test (arm at 90° abduction, 30° forward flexion, thumb down — resistance reveals weakness or pain).
🏥 Clinical — Rotator Cuff Tendinopathy and Tears Supraspinatus is the most commonly injured rotator cuff tendon — its avascular "critical zone" (~1 cm proximal to its insertion on the greater tubercle) is predisposed to degenerative change. Impingement occurs when the supraspinatus tendon is compressed between the humeral head and the coraco-acromial arch during abduction, particularly between 60–120° (the painful arc). Full-thickness tears are common in the over-50s; they are often treated with physiotherapy initially (strengthening infraspinatus and posterior cuff), with surgical repair for larger tears or failed conservative management.
Section 9

Other Glenohumeral Muscles

Deltoid — Three Parts

The deltoid is the prime mover of shoulder abduction and gives the shoulder its characteristic rounded contour. It originates from a continuous line across the lateral clavicle, acromion, and scapular spine, and all three parts converge to insert on the deltoid tuberosity of the humerus. The muscle is innervated by the axillary nerve (C5–C6) — the nerve winds around the surgical neck of the humerus in the quadrangular space.

PartOriginPrimary Actions
Anterior (clavicular) Lateral 1/3 of clavicle Flexion, abduction, internal rotation, horizontal adduction
Middle (acromial) Acromion Abduction (major abductor 15–90°)
Posterior (spinal) Spine of scapula Extension, external rotation, horizontal abduction
🧠 Deltoid vs Supraspinatus — An Important Distinction Students often confuse who does what in abduction. The supraspinatus initiates and stabilises (0–15°); the middle deltoid is the powerful abductor that takes over (15–90°); above 90° the upper trapezius + serratus anterior must rotate the scapula to bring the glenoid upward, allowing elevation to 180°. A patient with complete deltoid paralysis (axillary nerve injury) can still weakly abduct to ~15° using supraspinatus — but cannot reach above the shoulder.

Teres Major

Teres major originates from the inferior angle of the scapula and inserts onto the medial lip of the intertubercular groove. It acts alongside latissimus dorsi (extension, adduction, internal rotation) and shares its insertion point — hence the nickname "lat's little helper." It forms the inferior border of the quadrangular space and triangular interval. Innervated by the lower subscapular nerve (C5–C6).

Latissimus Dorsi

Latissimus dorsi is the broadest muscle of the back, arising from a wide origin (spinous processes T7–S5 via thoracolumbar fascia, iliac crest, inferior 3 ribs, and inferior angle of scapula). Its fibres converge and twist 180° to insert on the floor of the intertubercular groove. Actions: extension, adduction, and internal rotation of the shoulder. It is a powerful "pull-down" muscle critical for crutch walking, swimming, and climbing. Innervated by the thoracodorsal nerve (C6–C8), which is also at risk during axillary lymph node dissection.

Pectoralis Major

Pectoralis major has two heads — clavicular (medial half of clavicle) and sternocostal (sternum, costal cartilages 2–6). Both insert onto the lateral lip of the intertubercular groove. Overall actions: adduction, horizontal adduction, internal rotation; the clavicular head contributes to flexion. Innervated by both the medial and lateral pectoral nerves. The sternocostal head is more powerful. Rupture at the musculotendinous junction occurs in heavy bench press loading — clinically apparent as lateral chest wall asymmetry during resisted adduction.

Coracobrachialis

A small muscle originating from the coracoid process and inserting on the middle third of the medial humerus. It assists with shoulder flexion and adduction. Innervated by the musculocutaneous nerve (C5–C7), which actually passes through the belly of the muscle — a useful anatomical landmark when the nerve is at risk in surgery.

Neurovascular Supply

Key Nerves at the Shoulder

All shoulder muscles are supplied by the brachial plexus (C5–T1) or the accessory nerve (CN XI). The most clinically significant nerves are:

NerveRootMuscles SuppliedClinical Risk
Axillary nerve C5–C6 Deltoid, teres minor Surgical neck fracture, anterior GH dislocation, quadrangular space compression
Suprascapular nerve C5–C6 Supraspinatus, infraspinatus Suprascapular notch entrapment; ganglion cysts; overhead athletes
Upper subscapular nerve C5–C6 Subscapularis (upper) Brachial plexus injury
Lower subscapular nerve C5–C6 Subscapularis (lower), teres major Brachial plexus injury
Long thoracic nerve C5–C7 Serratus anterior Medial winging; surgery, traction injury, heavy backpack
Dorsal scapular nerve C5 Rhomboids, levator scapulae Thoracic outlet, scalene compression
Thoracodorsal nerve C6–C8 Latissimus dorsi Axillary lymph node dissection (breast cancer surgery)
Accessory nerve (CN XI) Cranial + C3–C4 Trapezius Posterior triangle surgery, IJV cannulation, lymph node biopsy

Arterial Supply

The shoulder is supplied primarily by the axillary artery and its branches, including the anterior and posterior circumflex humeral arteries (passing through the quadrangular space), the suprascapular artery, and the thoracoacromial trunk. A rich anastomotic network around the scapula (scapular anastomosis) provides collateral circulation if the axillary artery is occluded proximal to the scapular branches.

📌 The Quadrangular Space The quadrangular space is a gap in the posterior shoulder bounded by: teres minor (superior), teres major (inferior), long head of triceps (medial), and surgical neck of humerus (lateral). The axillary nerve and posterior circumflex humeral artery pass through it. Space-occupying lesions (fibrous bands, hypertrophied muscles) can compress these structures — "quadrangular space syndrome" — causing posterior shoulder pain and deltoid/teres minor weakness.

Clinical Summary

The shoulder is the most commonly injured joint region presenting to physiotherapy. The table below summarises the key conditions you must know, their anatomical basis, and what to look for clinically.
