1. Hip Bone (Coxal / Innominate Bone)

Slide 2 – Hip bone components

The hip bone is also known as the coxal bone or innominate bone. It is formed by the fusion of 3 bones:
  • Ilium — largest and most superior
  • Ischium — posterior and inferior
  • Pubis — anterior and inferior
These three bones fuse at the acetabulum.

4. Pubis

Slide 12 – Pubis body & rami

Slide 13 – Superior ramus features

Forms the anteroinferior part of the hip bone. Consists of a body + superior and inferior rami. Contributes to the anterior acetabulum and forms the anterior boundary of the obturator foramen.

StructureDescription
Body of pubisFlattened anteroposteriorly; medial surface = pubic symphysis
Pubic crestSuperior border of body; ends laterally at the pubic tubercle
Pubic tubercleLateral end of pubic crest; attachment of inguinal ligament

5. Ischium

Slide 14 – Ischium overview

Slide 15 – Ischial body, tuberosity, spine

Forms the posteroinferior part of the hip bone. Contributes to the posterior acetabulum and forms the posterior boundary of the obturator foramen.

StructureDescription
Ischial bodyBelow and behind the acetabulum
Ischial tuberosityInferior end of ischial body; weight-bearing in sitting; origin of hamstrings and adductor magnus
Ischial spinePosterior border projection; divides greater and lesser sciatic notches; attachment of sacrospinous ligament
Lesser sciatic notchBelow the ischial spine; transmits the pudendal nerve and obturator internus tendon
Ischial ramusProjects anteriorly to join inferior pubic ramus (forms ischiopubic ramus)
Clinical: The ischial tuberosity is the landmark for a posterior approach nerve block. Ischial bursitis ("weaver's bottom") causes sitting pain.

6. Acetabulum

Slide 16 – Acetabulum structure

Slide 17 – Lunate surface & acetabular fossa

Slide 18 – Acetabular labrum

A deep cup-shaped hemispherical cavity on the lateral aspect of the hip bone, facing obliquely forward, outward, and downward.

Composition

  • Ilium: upper 2/5
  • Pubis: anterior 1/5
  • Ischium: posterior 2/5

Key Structures

StructureDescription
Lunate surfaceC-shaped (horseshoe) articular surface; covered with hyaline cartilage; articulates with femoral head; occupies anterior, superior, and posterior parts
Acetabular fossaNon-articular rough depression in the floor; contains fat; continuous with the acetabular notch
Acetabular notchInferior deficiency in the rim; bridged by the transverse acetabular ligament; nutrient vessels enter here
Acetabular labrumFibrocartilaginous rim that deepens the acetabulum; holds the femoral head; extends across the notch as the transverse acetabular ligament
Clinical: Acetabular labral tears are a common cause of hip pain in active people — often presenting as groin pain with clicking.

7. Obturator Foramen

Slide 19 – Obturator foramen

  • Large gap in the hip bone, anteroinferior to the acetabulum, between the ischium and pubis
  • Shape: oval in males; triangular in females
  • Covered by the obturator membrane (attached to its margins)
  • The obturator groove (superior) allows passage of obturator vessels and nerve
