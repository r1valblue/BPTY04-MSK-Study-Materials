1. Clinical Relevance of the Ankle & Foot

Slide 1 – Title: Osteology Ankle & Foot

Slide 3 – Clinical relevance of the ankle & foot

The ankle and foot are foundational to all weight-bearing activities of daily life. Understanding their osteology is essential for physiotherapy because structure dictates both function and injury pattern.

Key functional roles of the ankle & foot:
  • Stability and mobility for different activities of daily living (ADLs)
  • Foot posture influences mechanics at the knee, hip, and lumbar spine
  • Injury can limit multiple movement planes simultaneously
  • One of the most frequently injured regions in sport

Slide 12 – Medial and lateral malleoli

Bimalleolar & Trimalleolar Fractures: The distal tibia and fibula are frequently fractured together. A "trimalleolar" fracture includes the medial malleolus, lateral malleolus, and posterior tibial margin ("posterior malleolus"). These are classified by the Weber or Lauge-Hansen systems and significantly affect ankle stability.

4. Talus

Slide 18 – Talus overview

Slide 19 – Talus articular surfaces

The talus is the keystone of the ankle. It sits between the distal tibia and fibula superiorly and the calcaneus inferiorly, transmitting the entire body weight from the leg to the foot. It is unique in that no muscles attach to it — all forces are transmitted through ligaments and articular surfaces.

FeatureDescriptionClinical Significance
Trochlea (dome)Superior convex articular surface; wider anteriorly than posteriorlyLocks into mortise in dorsiflexion (stable); looser in plantarflexion (sprain risk)
HeadAnterior convex surface; articulates with navicularSite of talonavicular joint (part of "triple joint complex")
NeckNarrow region between head and bodyCommon fracture site; poor blood supply → avascular necrosis risk
BodyContains the trochlea and posterior processHouses subtalar articulation with calcaneus
Subtalar facetsPosterior, middle, and anterior facets on inferior surfaceAllow subtalar inversion/eversion; key for terrain adaptation
No muscle attachmentsNo tendons insert directly onto talusBlood supply via ligaments; vulnerable to AVN after displaced fractures
Talar Fractures & AVN: Displaced talar neck fractures (Hawkins type II–III) disrupt the ligamentous blood supply. Because the talus has no muscle attachments to provide alternative vascularity, avascular necrosis rates can reach 20–50% for severely displaced fractures. Hawkins sign (subchondral lucency on X-ray at 6–8 weeks) indicates revascularisation and is a positive prognostic sign.
5. Calcaneus

Slide 20 – Calcaneus overview and function

Slide 21 – Calcaneus surfaces and landmarks

The calcaneus (heel bone) is the largest tarsal bone. It forms the posterior pillar of the longitudinal arch and is the primary contact point in heel-strike walking.

FeatureDescriptionClinical Significance
General shapeLargest tarsal bone; forms rearfootPrimary weight-bearing bone of the hindfoot
ArticulationsSuperiorly with talus (subtalar joint); anteriorly with cuboid (calcaneocuboid joint)Both joints contribute to the triple joint complex
Achilles insertionGastrocnemius and soleus via Achilles tendon insert on posterior calcaneal tuberosityLargest tendon in the body; Achilles tendinopathy, insertional or mid-portion
Plantar fascia originMedial calcaneal tubercle — plantar fascia and intrinsic foot muscles originate herePlantar fasciitis — pain at medial tubercle; worse first steps in morning
Sustentaculum taliMedial shelf supporting the talus; groove for flexor hallucis longus beneathLandmark for tarsal tunnel anatomy; FHL tendinopathy
Sinus tarsiLateral canal between calcaneus and talusSinus tarsi syndrome after lateral ankle sprains
Subtalar joint motion: The calcaneus moves beneath the fixed talus to produce subtalar inversion and eversion. This motion is critical for terrain adaptation and is assessed clinically via subtalar neutral position.
6. Navicular

Slide 22 – Navicular overview

Slide 23 – Navicular landmarks

FeatureDetail
LocationMedial side of midfoot; between talus proximally and cuneiforms distally
Navicular tuberosityProminent medial bony projection on the medial border — palpable landmark
Proximal articulationTalonavicular joint (concave navicular on convex talar head)
Distal articulationNaviculocuneiform joints — articulates with all three cuneiforms
Muscle insertionTibialis posterior — the ONLY muscle to insert directly on the navicular (at navicular tuberosity)
Navicular tuberosity & flat foot: The navicular tuberosity is the key bony landmark for measuring medial longitudinal arch height. A lowered navicular (navicular drop test) indicates excessive pronation / pes planus and is associated with medial tibial stress syndrome, plantar fasciitis, and hallux valgus. Tibialis posterior weakness is a primary cause of acquired flatfoot.
Navicular muscle insertion: "Only Tibialis Posterior talks to the Navicular." One muscle, one bone.

Lisfranc joint complex: The junction between the cuneiforms/cuboid and the metatarsal bases is the Lisfranc (tarsometatarsal) joint complex. The 2nd metatarsal base is recessed and "keystone" to this joint. A Lisfranc injury (ligament tear or fracture-dislocation) can destabilise the entire forefoot and is easily missed on plain X-ray.

Slide 27 – Cuboid

FeatureDetail

Proximal articulationCalcaneocuboid joint (part of triple joint complex)

Cuboid syndrome: Minor subluxation or disruption of the calcaneocuboid joint can cause lateral midfoot pain, especially in ballet dancers and runners. Often missed; responds well to manipulation (cuboid whip or squeeze technique).
9. Metatarsals

Slide 28 – Metatarsals

The five metatarsal bones form the forefoot framework, connecting the midfoot tarsals to the toes. Each has a proximal base, a shaft, a neck, and a distal head.

PartDescriptionClinical Note
BaseProximal widened end; articulates with tarsals5th metatarsal base (styloid process) — common avulsion fracture site
ShaftLong slender bodyStress fractures common in runners and military recruits (especially 2nd metatarsal)
NeckDistal end of shaft proximal to head5th metatarsal neck — Jones fracture site (poor healing due to watershed blood supply)
HeadDistal convex surface; articulates with proximal phalanxMetatarsophalangeal (MTP) joint; hallux valgus at 1st MTP; Morton's neuroma between 3rd–4th heads
1st vs. 5th metatarsal: The 1st metatarsal is the thickest and bears the most load at toe-off. The 5th metatarsal has two clinically important fracture zones: the styloid avulsion (Zone 1, good healing) and the Jones fracture at the metaphyseal-diaphyseal junction (Zone 2, poor healing due to limited blood supply).
10. Phalanges

Slide 29 – Phalanges

Slide 30 – Let's practice!

FeatureDetail
Total number14 phalanges across 5 toes
Toes 2–53 phalanges each: proximal, intermediate (middle), and distal
1st toe (hallux)Only 2 phalanges: proximal and distal — no intermediate
JointsMTP (metatarsophalangeal), PIP (proximal interphalangeal), DIP (distal interphalangeal)
Sesamoid bonesTwo sesamoid bones beneath 1st MTP joint (within flexor hallucis brevis tendons) — bear weight during push-off
Hallux deformities: Hallux valgus is lateral deviation of the 1st toe at the MTP joint, associated with a bunion (medial prominence). Hallux rigidus is arthritis of the 1st MTP — reduced dorsiflexion, painful push-off, compensated by external rotation of the limb. Both have significant functional consequences for gait and balance.
Foot bones summary (proximal → distal):
Calcaneus + Talus → Navicular + Cuboid → 3 Cuneiforms → 5 Metatarsals → 14 Phalanges
