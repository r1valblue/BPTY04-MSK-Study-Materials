  • Identify and classify joints according to their structure, mobility, and range of motion
  • Describe the key anatomical features of synovial joints
  • Relate joint structure to functional movement capacity
An articulation (joint) is the connection between two bones in the skeleton. The joint is the centre of motion of a bone in relation to another.

Joints are classified by: (1) Structure, (2) Mobility, and (3) Range of Motion

1. Classification by Structure

Slide 4 – Structural classification overview

C. Synovial Joints

The most common joint type in the human body. Freely mobile.

Slide 13 – Synovial joint features

Slide 14 – Additional synovial features

Slide 15 – Types of synovial joints

Characteristic Features:

  • Articular Capsule
    • Outer fibrous layer — stabilises the joint
    • Inner synovial layer (synovial membrane) — absorbs and secretes synovial fluid to lubricate the joint cavity
  • Articular Surface — covered in hyaline (articular) cartilage to reduce friction and provide shock absorption

Additional Features:

  • Articular discs — e.g., medial and lateral menisci in the knee
  • Bursae — small sacs lined by synovial membrane, filled with synovial fluid; reduce friction caused by muscles and tendons over bony prominences

Types of Synovial Joint:

Ball & Socket · Hinge · Pivot · Condylar (Ellipsoid) · Saddle · Plane

3. Classification by Range of Motion (Axes)

Slide 17 – ROM classification overview

A. Multiaxial — Ball & Socket Joint

Slide 18 – Ball & socket joint

  • One ball-shaped and one socket-like articular surface
  • Examples: acetabulofemoral joint (hip), glenohumeral joint (shoulder)
  • Movements: Flexion/Extension (frontal axis) · Abduction/Adduction (sagittal axis) · Internal/External Rotation (vertical axis)

D. Plane Joint (Non-axial)

Slide 23 – Plane joint

  • Movement along the plane of the articular surface, not around an axis — cannot be classified as uniaxial/biaxial/multiaxial
  • Movement: gliding or sliding
  • Examples: acromioclavicular joint, spinal zygapophyseal joints

4. Summary Exercise & Self-Test

Slide 24 – Questions

Slide 25 – Answers

Q1. Do all articulations have a synovial membrane?

No — only synovial joints have a synovial membrane. Fibrous and cartilaginous joints lack one.

Q2. Which is usually more mobile — synarthroses or amphiarthroses?

Amphiarthroses (slightly mobile, e.g., pubic symphysis, syndesmosis) > Synarthroses (immobile, e.g., skull sutures, gomphoses, synchondroses).

Q3. What are the most mobile articulations of the human body?

Diarthroses — freely mobile joints, i.e., every synovial joint.

Q4. What is the movement difference between an Ellipsoid/Condylar and a Saddle joint?

Both are biaxial. The saddle joint also permits circumduction (a combination movement), whereas the condylar/ellipsoid joint does not. The saddle joint has a greater range due to its reciprocally curved surfaces (convex meets concave).
