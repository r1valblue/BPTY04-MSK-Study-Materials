1. Trunk Overview

Slide 3 – Trunk composition and function

Slide 1 – Title slide: Trunk Anatomy – Osteology

The trunk forms the axial skeleton of the body and is composed of three major structures:

  • Spinal column — the central articulating column of vertebrae
  • Sternum — the flat anterior bony plate of the thorax
  • Ribs — 12 paired curved bones forming the thoracic cage
Functions of the trunk skeleton:
  • Support — bears the weight of the head, neck, and upper limbs
  • Movement — allows flexion, extension, lateral flexion, and rotation
  • Load transfer — transmits forces between upper and lower limbs
  • Protection — encloses and protects the spinal cord, heart, lungs, and abdominal organs

The adult spinal column has four alternating curves in the sagittal plane:

CurveDirectionTypeNote
CervicalLordosis (anterior convex)SecondaryDevelops when infant lifts head

LumbarLordosis (anterior convex)SecondaryDevelops with upright posture/walking

Primary vs Secondary Curves: Primary curves (thoracic, sacral) are present at birth. Secondary curves (cervical, lumbar) develop postnatally in response to gravity and muscular development. Exaggeration of lumbar lordosis or thoracic kyphosis has important postural and clinical implications.

4. Orientation of a Vertebra

Slide 9 – Orientation rules

Two rules for orienting an isolated vertebra:
  • Vertebral bodies are anterior — the larger, cylindrical weight-bearing mass faces forward
  • Spinous processes are posterior — the pointed/bladed projection faces the back

Once anterior/posterior is established, superior/inferior can be determined by facet orientation and body height. Regional identification requires knowledge of the specific features covered in the sections below.

5. Cervical Vertebrae (C1–C7)

Atlas — C1

Slide 14 – Atlas (C1)

The atlas is unique in having no vertebral body — it is a bony ring with two lateral masses and two arches. Key features:

  • Anterior and posterior arches (no body, no spinous process)
  • Two large superior articular facets (kidney-shaped, concave) → articulate with occipital condyles of skull (atlantooccipital joint)
  • Two inferior articular facets → articulate with C2 (atlantoaxial joint)
  • Transverse ligament groove on inner anterior arch → holds odontoid process (dens) of C2
  • Transverse foramina bilaterally → passage for vertebral artery, vein, sympathetic nerve

Axis — C2

Slide 15 – Axis (C2)

The axis is defined by its distinctive odontoid process (dens) — a tooth-like superior projection that acts as a pivot for atlas rotation.

  • Dens = embryological remnant of C1 vertebral body, fused to C2
  • Atlantoaxial joint (C1–C2) allows ~50% of all cervical rotation (approximately 40–45° each side)
  • Dens held in place by transverse ligament of the atlas — rupture is life-threatening
  • Has a proper spinous process (bifid) and transverse foramina
Clinical Note — Dens Fractures: Jefferson fracture (C1 burst) and Type II dens fractures (at the dens base) are the most common upper cervical injuries. Both can compress the spinal cord. The "hangman's fracture" involves bilateral C2 pedicle fractures.

C3–C6 (Typical Cervical)

Slide 16 – C3–C6 cervical vertebra features

  • Small, wide vertebral body with uncinate processes (uncovertebral joints/joints of Luschka)
  • Bifid spinous process (C3–C6) — forked tip
  • Transverse foramen — transmits vertebral artery (C1–C6), vein, sympathetic plexus
  • Transverse process has anterior and posterior tubercles
  • Articular facets oriented at ~45° to horizontal → allows flexion/extension AND rotation
  • Largest vertebral foramen (triangular) relative to body size → accommodates cervical spinal cord enlargement

C7 — Vertebra Prominens

Slide 17 – C7 vertebra prominens

  • Transitional vertebra between cervical and thoracic
  • Long, non-bifid spinous process — the most prominent posterior cervical landmark (vertebra prominens)
  • Transverse foramen present but does not transmit vertebral artery (artery enters at C6)
  • Body larger than C3–C6; begins transition to thoracic morphology
Mnemonic — C7 at a glance: "C7 Sticks Out" — the long, solitary (non-bifid) spinous process of C7 is palpable through the skin and is the standard landmark for counting vertebral levels.

6. Thoracic Vertebrae (T1–T12)

Slide 18 – Thoracic vertebra

Thoracic vertebrae are uniquely identified by their costal (rib) facets — articular surfaces for rib head articulation.

FeatureDescription
Vertebral body shapeHeart-shaped; medium size; costal demi-facets on superior/inferior body margins
Costal facetsSuperior + inferior demi-facets on body (for rib heads); facet on transverse process (for rib tubercle)
Spinous processLong, slender, steeply angled inferiorly — overlaps the vertebra below (especially T5–T8)
Transverse processesPoint posterolaterally; bear articular facets for costal tubercle at T1–T10
Articular facetsOriented 60–80° from horizontal; 20° from frontal plane → mainly allows rotation, limits flexion
Vertebral foramenCircular, smaller than cervical — spinal cord is narrower here
Rib–vertebra relationships: Each rib typically articulates with TWO vertebrae — the demi-facet on the body of its own numbered vertebra AND the demi-facet on the body above. For example, rib 6 articulates with T5 (inferior demi-facet) and T6 (superior demi-facet), plus the transverse process of T6.

7. Lumbar Vertebrae (L1–L5)

Slide 19 – Lumbar vertebra

Lumbar vertebrae are the largest of the mobile vertebrae, reflecting their role in bearing the combined weight of the head, trunk, and upper limbs.

FeatureDescription
Vertebral bodyKidney-shaped; largest of all vertebrae; broad and tall
Spinous processBroad, thick, hatchet-shaped; projects horizontally posteriorly
Transverse processesSlender; homologous to ribs (costal element); true transverse process = accessory process
Articular facetsConcave/convex (interlocking); oriented nearly sagittally → allows flexion/extension, resists rotation
Vertebral foramenTriangular; smallest relative foramen (narrower canal → clinical significance)
Mammillary processSmall posterior bump on superior articular process; muscle attachment
Clinical Note — Lumbar Canal Stenosis: The triangular, smallest vertebral foramen in the lumbar spine makes it vulnerable to stenosis from disc prolapse, osteophytes, or ligamentum flavum hypertrophy. L4/L5 and L5/S1 are the most clinically involved levels.

9. Sacrum & Coccyx

Slide 25 – Sacrum and coccyx overview

Slide 26 – Sacrum views

Slide 27 – Sacrum and coccyx detail

Sacrum

The sacrum is a large, triangular bone formed by the fusion of 5 sacral vertebrae (S1–S5), typically completing fusion by early adulthood. It forms the posterior wall of the pelvis and articulates laterally with the ilium at the sacroiliac joints (SIJ).

Surface / FeatureDescription
Base (superior)Wide upper surface; articulates with L5 at lumbosacral joint; sacral promontory = anterior leading edge
Apex (inferior)Narrow inferior tip; articulates with coccyx
Pelvic (anterior) surfaceConcave; smooth; anterior sacral foramina (×4 pairs) → anterior rami of S1–S4 exit
Dorsal (posterior) surfaceConvex, roughened; posterior sacral foramina → posterior rami exit; median sacral crest (fused spinous processes)
Lateral surface (ala)Auricular surface (ear-shaped) → articulates with ilium at SIJ
Sacral canalContinuation of vertebral canal; contains cauda equina and filum terminale
Sacral hiatusInferior opening of sacral canal where laminae of S4/S5 fail to fuse → site for caudal epidural injections
Sacral cornuaInferior articular processes of S5 flanking the hiatus; palpable landmarks

Coccyx

The coccyx (tailbone) consists of 3–4 fused vertebral rudiments. Although small, it serves as an important attachment site:

  • Attachment for coccygeus and levator ani muscles (pelvic floor)
  • Attachment for the sacrotuberous and sacrospinous ligaments (via coccygeal elements)
  • Filum terminale dura mater attaches to the coccyx
Clinical Note — Coccydynia: Pain at the coccyx (coccydynia) often follows a fall or childbirth injury. Physiotherapy management includes posture education, tailbone-relieving cushions, internal or external manual therapy, and pelvic floor rehabilitation.

10. Sternum

Slide 29 – Sternum

The sternum is a flat, elongated bone forming the anterior midline of the thoracic cage. It consists of three parts:

PartLocationKey Landmarks / Articulations
ManubriumSuperiormostJugular (suprasternal) notch superiorly; clavicular notches (sternoclavicular joints); first costal cartilage; manubriosternal joint (with body)
Body (Gladiolus)Middle, longest partCostal notches for ribs 2–7; fused from four sternebrae
Xiphoid processInferiormostCartilaginous in youth, ossifies with age; attachment for rectus abdominis and diaphragm
Sternal Angle (Angle of Louis): The manubriosternal joint creates a palpable ridge — the sternal angle. This is a crucial surface anatomy landmark:
  • Marks the articulation of the 2nd costal cartilage (rib counting starts here)
  • Level of the T4/T5 intervertebral disc posteriorly
  • Division between superior and inferior mediastinum
  • Bifurcation of the trachea (carina)
Clinical Note — Sternotomy & CPR: The sternum is a commonly used surgical access route (median sternotomy) for cardiac procedures. During CPR, compressions are applied to the lower half of the sternal body to avoid the xiphoid process, which can fracture and lacerate the liver.

Clinical Note — Rib Fractures: Ribs 4–9 are most commonly fractured (exposed, less protected by musculature). First rib fractures suggest major trauma (associated with subclavian vessel and brachial plexus injury). Floating rib (11–12) injuries can damage the kidney. Multiple rib fractures causing paradoxical chest movement = flail chest.
