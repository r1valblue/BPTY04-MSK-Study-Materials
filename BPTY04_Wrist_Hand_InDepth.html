1 — Osteology: Bones of the Wrist & Hand

1.1 Carpal Bones

The wrist contains eight carpal bones arranged in two rows. They are irregular bones whose complexity reflects the enormous variety of wrist motion required for daily function. The carpals collectively form a concave palmar arch — this architectural feature is important because it creates the floor and walls of the carpal tunnel.

Proximal Row (Superior)

Scaphoid Lunate Triquetrum Pisiform

Distal Row (Inferior)

Trapezium Trapezoid Capitate Hamate
Mnemonic — Carpal Bones From slides (Kaewlai et al., 2008):
Proximal row — "She Likes To Play" → Scaphoid, Lunate, Triquetrum, Pisiform
Distal row — "Try To Catch Her" → Trapezium, Trapezoid, Capitate, Hamate
Key Concept The pisiform is a sesamoid bone — it develops within the tendon of Flexor Carpi Ulnaris and sits anterior to the triquetrum. Because it is a sesamoid, it does not directly participate in the radiocarpal joint. The hamate has a distinctive hook-shaped projection on its palmar surface (the "hook of hamate") which forms the medial wall of the carpal tunnel and serves as an attachment for the flexor retinaculum.
Slide 4 — Carpal bones, rows & mnemonics

The scaphoid bridges both rows — it is the largest carpal bone and it spans the proximal and distal rows, helping coordinate intercarpal motion. This bridge position makes it the most commonly fractured carpal bone, as it absorbs substantial compressive force during a fall on an outstretched hand (FOOSH).

Clinical — Scaphoid Fracture Most commonly fractured carpal bone, typically from FOOSH. The hallmark clinical sign is pain and tenderness in the anatomical snuffbox. The key danger is avascular necrosis (AVN): the scaphoid receives its blood supply distally, entering at the waist from the radial artery branches. A fracture at the waist therefore cuts off supply to the proximal fragment, which may die. Never dismiss wrist pain after FOOSH as a sprain — if X-ray is negative but snuffbox tenderness persists, treat as a scaphoid fracture and arrange MRI/CT. Untreated scaphoid non-union leads to progressive wrist osteoarthritis.

1.2 Metacarpals

There are five metacarpal bones (I–V), each consisting of a base, shaft, and head. Metacarpal I is the shortest and sturdiest (supporting the thumb); metacarpal III is the longest. The medial and lateral surfaces of each shaft are concave — this concavity provides attachment for the interosseous muscles.

Clinical — Metacarpal Fractures Boxer's Fracture: Fracture of the 5th metacarpal neck, caused by punching a hard surface with a clenched fist. The distal fragment angulates palmarly, causing apparent shortening of the little finger. Usually managed conservatively unless angulation is severe.

Bennett's Fracture: Fracture-dislocation of the base of the 1st metacarpal. Forced hyperabduction of the thumb drives the metacarpal base off the trapezium. The pull of Abductor Pollicis Longus subluxes the main metacarpal fragment radially and proximally. Often requires surgical fixation due to joint involvement and instability.

1.3 Phalanges

The thumb has two phalanges (proximal and distal). Each finger (digits 2–5) has three phalanges: proximal, middle, and distal. Each phalanx consists of a base (proximal), shaft, and head (distal). The distal phalanx terminates as the tuft — a broader bony end that supports the fingertip pulp and nail bed.

2 — Arthrology: Joints of the Wrist & Hand Complex

2.1 Distal Radioulnar Joint (DRUJ)

The DRUJ is a pivot (trochoid) joint — uniplanar motion only. The convex head of the ulna articulates with the ulnar notch of the radius. During pronation and supination, the radius rotates around the fixed ulna. Together with the proximal radioulnar joint (at the elbow), the DRUJ allows forearm rotation of approximately 85° in each direction.

The DRUJ is stabilised by the distal radioulnar ligaments (the palmar and dorsal components of the TFCC — see Section 3) and the interosseous membrane. The interosseous membrane is crucial: it transmits forces from the hand up through the forearm — roughly 80% via the radius and 20% via the ulna, with force redistribution possible due to the membrane.

2.2 Radiocarpal Joint (Wrist Joint)

The radiocarpal joint is a condyloid (ellipsoid) synovial joint, allowing motion in two planes (biplanar). The articulating surfaces are:

  • Proximally: the distal articular surface of the radius plus the articular disc of the TFCC
  • Distally: the proximal carpal row — scaphoid, lunate, and triquetrum (the pisiform does not articulate here)
Key Concept — Why not the ulna? The ulna does not directly articulate with the carpal bones. Instead, the TFCC articular disc interposed between the ulnar head and the triquetrum/lunate extends the functional joint surface. This is why the disc is so important — it effectively lengthens the proximal articulating surface and cushions the ulnar side of the wrist.

Ligaments of the Radiocarpal Joint

LigamentOrigin → InsertionFunction
Palmar (Anterior) Radiocarpal Anterior radius → scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum Stability in sagittal plane; ensures hand follows forearm into supination (prevents lag)
Dorsal (Posterior) Radiocarpal Posterior radius → scaphoid, lunate, triquetrum Stability in sagittal plane; ensures hand follows forearm into pronation
Radial Collateral Radial styloid → scaphoid Prevents ulnar deviation (adduction) — keeps wrist from "falling" medially
Ulnar Collateral Ulnar styloid → triquetrum & pisiform Prevents radial deviation (abduction) — keeps wrist from "falling" laterally
Concept — Collateral Ligament Logic Think of collateral ligaments as preventing the motion that would tighten them on the opposite side. The radial collateral runs on the radial (lateral) side, so it prevents the wrist from deviating away from radial — i.e. it prevents ulnar deviation. The ulnar collateral runs on the ulnar (medial) side, preventing radial deviation. This is a consistent pattern across all collateral ligaments in the upper limb.

Wrist Movements and Producing Muscles

MovementRangePrimary Muscles
Flexion (palmar flexion) ~80° FCU, FCR, FDS, FDP, FPL

Concept — Why ulnar deviation is greater The radial styloid extends further distally than the ulnar styloid, creating a bony block that limits radial deviation to ~20°. The ulnar styloid is shorter, giving more room for ulnar deviation (~35°). This is why ulnar deviation during writing is so natural.

2.3 Midcarpal Joints

The midcarpal joint is the articulation between the proximal and distal carpal rows. It is technically a compound joint with two articulatory patterns:

  • Ellipsoidal (condyloid) portion: the head of the capitate and hamate articulating with the concave surface of the scaphoid and lunate — this component allows flexion/extension.
  • Plane joint portion: between the scaphoid-trapezium-trapezoid on the radial side — allows small gliding movements.

Approximately half of wrist flexion and half of extension occurs at the midcarpal joint, with the other half at the radiocarpal joint. Stabilising ligaments include the palmar and dorsal intercarpal ligaments, which resist excessive flexion and extension respectively and coordinate intercarpal motion.

2.4 Carpometacarpal (CMC) Joints

1st CMC Joint (Thumb) — Saddle Joint

The 1st CMC is the most mobile and mechanically complex joint in the hand. It is a saddle (sellar) joint between the trapezium and the base of metacarpal I. The saddle configuration means each articular surface is convex in one direction and concave in the perpendicular direction — allowing motion in two planes (frontal and sagittal) plus a degree of rotation, which together produce the key movement of opposition.

What makes opposition special? Opposition is the combined movement of flexion, medial rotation, and palmar abduction at the 1st CMC — it brings the thumb pad across to meet the pad of the little finger. This is uniquely human and enables precision grip. The ability to oppose is driven primarily by Opponens Pollicis (median nerve) and assisted by FPB, APB.
Ligament GroupRole
Dorsal ligamentsPrimary stabilisers — prevent dorsal displacement during pinch, grip, and opposition
Palmar (volar) ligamentsLimit extension; guide flexion and rotation during opposition
Lateral ligamentsSide-to-side stability; limit abduction and adduction

2nd–5th CMC Joints — Plane Joints

The CMC joints of fingers II–V are plane (gliding) joints with very limited mobility. The 2nd and 3rd CMC are essentially immobile (forming a rigid central pillar of the hand). The 4th and 5th have slightly more glide, allowing the little finger side of the palm to cup — important for power grip and cupping the hand.

2.5 Metacarpophalangeal (MCP) Joints

The MCP joints are condyloid (ellipsoid) joints — biplanar. The convex head of each metacarpal articulates with the concave base of the proximal phalanx. This allows flexion/extension plus abduction/adduction (spreading the fingers). The design trades stability for mobility, which is why MCP joints can be susceptible to injury during ball sports.

Stabilising Structures of the MCP

StructureRole
Collateral ligamentsPrevent lateral (side-to-side) displacement; tighten in flexion. This is critical clinically — always splint MCPs in flexion to prevent ligament shortening.
Accessory collateral ligamentsConnect the collateral ligaments to the palmar plate; help guide movement
Palmar plate (volar plate)Fibrocartilaginous thickening of the joint capsule on the palmar side — prevents hyperextension and increases joint surface area
Deep transverse metacarpal ligamentConnects palmar plates of digits 2–5; maintains metacarpal spacing and stability during grip
Clinical — Gamekeeper's/Skier's Thumb The ulnar collateral ligament of the 1st MCP is commonly torn by forced radial deviation of the thumb (e.g. falling on a ski pole — hence "skier's thumb"). If the ligament is avulsed and folds back on itself (Stener lesion), it cannot heal without surgery. Test by applying radial stress to the MCP in both extension and 30° flexion — instability suggests UCL rupture.

2.6 Interphalangeal (IP) Joints

The IP joints are hinge (ginglymus) joints — uniplanar, moving only in the sagittal plane (flexion/extension). Their close-packed (most stable) position is full extension. There are two types:

  • Proximal interphalangeal (PIP): between proximal and middle phalanges (digits 2–5). This is the most important joint for grip — PIP flexion contractures are disabling.
  • Distal interphalangeal (DIP): between middle and distal phalanges (digits 2–5).
  • The thumb has a single IP joint between its proximal and distal phalanges.
Clinical — Jersey Finger & Mallet Finger Mallet finger: Rupture of the extensor tendon at the DIP joint, causing the distal phalanx to droop into flexion. Common in ball sports — the ball strikes the fingertip. Treated with 6–8 weeks of DIP extension splinting.

Jersey finger: Avulsion of the FDP tendon from the distal phalanx, typically the ring finger during rugby (grabbing a jersey). Causes loss of active DIP flexion. Requires surgical repair.

3 — The Triangular Fibrocartilage Complex (TFCC)

The TFCC is a load-bearing, stabilising fibrocartilaginous structure on the ulnar side of the wrist. It is not a single structure but a complex of three components working together:

ComponentStructureFunction
Triangular fibrocartilage disc Fibrocartilaginous disc between ulnar head and proximal carpal row Extends the proximal articulating surface medially; transmits and absorbs compressive forces from the ulnar side of the wrist (approx. 20% of axial load)
Radioulnar ligaments Palmar and dorsal components spanning radius to ulna Primary stabilisers of the DRUJ — prevent excessive rotation and translation of the ulna on the radius
Ulnocarpal ligaments Ulnolunate and ulnotriquetral bands Support the ulnar carpus; limit ulnar deviation and prevent excessive dorsal translation
Clinical — TFCC Tears TFCC injuries are common in racquet sports and gymnastics (repetitive loading/rotation). They present as ulnar-sided wrist pain — often described as a "deep ache" worsened by forearm rotation and gripping. The press test (patient pushes up from a chair using the affected wrist) and piano key sign (ballottement of the ulnar head) can aid diagnosis. MRI arthrography is the gold standard investigation. Peripheral tears may heal conservatively; central tears (avascular zone) often need arthroscopic debridement.

4 — The Carpal Tunnel

The carpal tunnel is a narrow osseofibrous passageway on the anterior (palmar) aspect of the wrist. Its tight dimensions are precisely why it is clinically relevant.

4.1 Borders

WallFormed by
Floor & sides (carpal arch)Laterally: scaphoid tubercle + trapezium tubercle
Medially: pisiform + hook of hamate
RoofFlexor retinaculum (transverse carpal ligament) — thick fibrous band spanning between the medial and lateral bony prominences
Landmark The entrance of the carpal tunnel aligns with the distal wrist crease. You can find it on yourself — run your finger along the distal crease and you are marking the proximal opening of the tunnel.

4.2 Contents — The 9+1 Rule

The tunnel contains nine tendons and one nerve:

ContentsDetail
FPL tendon (×1)Surrounded by its own synovial sheath (the radial bursa)
FDS tendons (×4)To digits 2–5; share a common synovial sheath (the ulnar bursa) with FDP
FDP tendons (×4)To digits 2–5; share the common ulnar bursa
Median nerveMost superficial and lateral — lies just beneath the flexor retinaculum
Memory Aid — What's NOT in the carpal tunnel The FCR tendon runs within the substance of the flexor retinaculum itself (in its own groove) — not inside the tunnel. The ulnar nerve and artery pass through Guyon's canal (ulnar canal) — a separate fibro-osseous tunnel medial to the carpal tunnel. They are not compressed in CTS.
Key Concept — Palmar Cutaneous Branch of Median Nerve This branch leaves the median nerve proximal to the carpal tunnel and travels superficial to the flexor retinaculum to supply the skin over the thenar eminence. This is why carpal tunnel syndrome does not produce numbness over the thenar skin — only over the palmar digits.

4.3 Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS)

CTS is the most common mononeuropathy — caused by compression of the median nerve within the tunnel. It may be idiopathic but is also associated with pregnancy, hypothyroidism, rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes, and repetitive wrist use. Any increase in tunnel content volume (synovitis, oedema, ganglion) or decrease in tunnel volume (fracture deformity) can precipitate compression.

FeatureDetail
SymptomsNumbness, tingling, and burning in the lateral 3½ digits (thumb, index, middle, radial half of ring). Classic: nocturnal symptoms — patients wake to shake their hand ("flick sign").
Tinel's signTap over the carpal tunnel → reproduces tingling in median nerve distribution

5 — The Anatomical Snuffbox

The anatomical snuffbox is a triangular depression visible on the dorsolateral aspect of the wrist when the thumb is extended and abducted. Its name derives from its historical use as a surface to hold snuff powder.

BorderStructure
Lateral (radial) borderAbductor Pollicis Longus + Extensor Pollicis Brevis (in front)
Medial (ulnar) borderExtensor Pollicis Longus (behind)
Proximal borderRadial styloid process
FloorScaphoid + trapezium (distal)

Contents

Running through the snuffbox: Radial artery (passes deep to EPL on its way to the dorsal hand and deep palmar arch), branches of the radial nerve (superficial branch — sensory), and the cephalic vein (originates here as the dorsal venous network coalesces).

Clinical Relevance Tenderness in the anatomical snuffbox after FOOSH = scaphoid fracture until proven otherwise. The scaphoid forms the floor of the snuffbox, and direct palpation compresses it against the radial artery and periosteum. Tenderness at this site is more sensitive than X-ray in the acute setting — X-ray may be negative for up to 2 weeks after a true scaphoid fracture.

6 — Extrinsic Muscles of the Thumb & Fingers

6.1 Extrinsic Thumb Muscles (Pollicis Group)

Four muscles originate in the forearm and act on the thumb. Three run through the anatomical snuffbox (APL, EPB, EPL); FPL runs through the carpal tunnel.

MuscleOriginInsertionActionNerve
Flexor Pollicis Longus (FPL) Anterior surface of radius + interosseous membrane Palmar surface of distal phalanx I IP I: flexion; MCP I: flexion; CMC I: opposition; wrist: flexion & abduction Median (AIN)
Abductor Pollicis Longus (APL) Dorsal radius + ulna + interosseous membrane Base of metacarpal I CMC I: abduction; wrist: flexion & abduction (radial deviation) Radial (PIN)
Extensor Pollicis Brevis (EPB) Dorsal radius + interosseous membrane Base of proximal phalanx I CMC I + MCP I: extension; wrist: extension & abduction Radial (PIN)
Extensor Pollicis Longus (EPL) Dorsal ulna + interosseous membrane Base of distal phalanx I MCP I + IP I: extension; CMC I: adduction; wrist: extension & abduction Radial (PIN)
Concept — APL & EPB vs EPL APL and EPB share the 1st extensor compartment and form the anterior boundary of the anatomical snuffbox. EPL alone forms the posterior boundary, hooking around Lister's tubercle on the radius to change direction. EPL is the muscle most vulnerable to spontaneous rupture in rheumatoid arthritis — the inflamed extensor retinaculum at Lister's tubercle erodes the tendon, causing sudden loss of IP thumb extension.
Clinical — De Quervain's Tenosynovitis Stenosing tenosynovitis of the APL and EPB tendons in the 1st extensor compartment. Common in new mothers (lifting babies with radially deviated wrists). Presents as pain and swelling over the radial styloid. Finkelstein's test: patient clasps the thumb within the fist and the examiner ulnarly deviates the wrist — reproduction of sharp pain is positive.

6.2 Extrinsic Finger Muscles

Two muscles originating in the forearm extend the fingers independently:

MuscleOriginInsertionActionNerve
Extensor Digiti Minimi (EDM) Lateral epicondyle of humerus Middle & distal phalanges V (via dorsal aponeurosis) MCP/PIP/DIP V: extension & abduction; wrist: extension & ulnar deviation Radial (PIN)
Extensor Indicis (EI) Dorsal ulna + interosseous membrane Dorsal aponeurosis of index finger (II) II: extension & adduction; wrist: extension & abduction Radial (PIN)
Why do these two exist? The main finger extensor (EDC — Extensor Digitorum Communis) extends all four fingers but its tendons are linked by juncturae tendinum, limiting independent extension. EI (index) and EDM (little finger) provide independent extension for the most-used "pointing" fingers. When you point (extend only the index), you are using EI while EDC is partially pulled back by the juncturae.

7 — Intrinsic Muscles of the Hand
Extrinsic vs Intrinsic Extrinsic muscles originate in the forearm — they control gross grip strength. Intrinsic muscles originate and insert entirely within the hand — they control fine motor function: precision pinch, independent finger movement, spreading/closing fingers, and thumb opposition. Intrinsic paralysis (e.g. from ulnar nerve palsy) devastates hand dexterity despite preserved grip.

7.1 Thenar Muscles

The thenar eminence (the fleshy mound at the base of the thumb) is formed by three muscles, all innervated by the recurrent branch of the median nerve (with one exception noted). Remember them as "OAF" — Opponens, Abductor, Flexor — from deep to superficial.

MuscleOriginInsertionActionNerve
Opponens Pollicis Tubercle of trapezium + flexor retinaculum Lateral margin of metacarpal I (entire length) CMC I: opposition (medial rotation + flexion of metacarpal) Median (recurrent branch)
Abductor Pollicis Brevis (APB) Scaphoid, trapezium tubercles + flexor retinaculum Lateral base of proximal phalanx I CMC I: abduction (pulls thumb anteriorly away from palm) Median (recurrent branch)
Flexor Pollicis Brevis (FPB) Superficial head: flexor retinaculum / Deep head: capitate + trapezium Base of proximal phalanx I CMC I: flexion & opposition; MCP I: flexion Superficial head: Median; Deep head: Ulnar
Memory — Thenar Muscles "Occasionally A Fox" (lateral to medial, or deep to superficial): Opponens Pollicis, Abductor Pollicis Brevis, Flexor Pollicis Brevis. All three are median nerve — except the deep head of FPB (ulnar). In a median nerve lesion at the wrist (CTS), thenar wasting develops — you will specifically see flattening of the thenar eminence and the patient cannot oppose the thumb to the little finger.
Clinical — "Ape Hand" Deformity Median nerve palsy at the wrist causes loss of thenar muscles → the thumb falls flat into the plane of the palm (loss of opposition/abduction). This produces the "ape thumb" or "simian hand" appearance — the palm looks flat with the thumb lying beside rather than in front. This also impairs the precision grip needed for most daily tasks.

7.2 Adductor Pollicis

Technically part of the thenar group but listed separately — it lies deep to the thenar muscles and is the only thenar muscle innervated by the ulnar nerve (deep branch).

HeadOrigin
Oblique headCapitate + bases of metacarpals II & III
Transverse headPalmar surface of metacarpal III

Insertion: Both heads → medial base of proximal phalanx I (via the ulnar sesamoid).
Action: Adducts the thumb (pulls it back into the plane of the palm); assists opposition and MCP flexion.

Concept — Froment's Sign The radial artery passes between the two heads of Adductor Pollicis to become the deep palmar arch. When the ulnar nerve is injured, the patient cannot adduct the thumb — instead they compensate using FPL (median nerve) to hold a piece of paper, causing the IP joint to flex. This is Froment's sign — a classic test for ulnar nerve palsy.

7.3 Hypothenar Muscles

The hypothenar eminence (the pad on the ulnar side of the palm at the base of the little finger) mirrors the thenar eminence in organisation. All hypothenar muscles are innervated by the ulnar nerve (deep branch).

7.4 Lumbricals

The four lumbricals are unique muscles that originate from tendons rather than bone. They link the flexor system to the extensor system and are critical for the hand's most refined movement.

FeatureLumbricals I & II (Lateral)Lumbricals III & IV (Medial)
OriginFrom radial side of FDP tendons of digits 2 & 3 (unipennate)From adjacent sides of FDP tendons of digits 3–5 (bipennate)
InsertionLateral extensor hood of each digit (joins the extensor expansion)
ActionMCP joints 2–5: flexion; IP joints 2–5: extension
InnervationMedian nerveUlnar nerve (deep branch)
Why is the lumbrical action so unusual? Most muscles can only flex OR extend joints. The lumbrical achieves both simultaneously because it attaches to the FDP tendon proximally and the extensor hood distally. When it contracts, it does two things at once: it slackens the FDP (reducing IP flexion force) while tensioning the extensor hood (producing IP extension). The net result is the "intrinsic plus" position: MCP flexion + IP extension. This is the position needed to pick up a glass or type on a keyboard.
Clinical — Clawing Loss of lumbricals (and interossei) leads to the "intrinsic minus" or claw hand posture: MCP hyperextension + IP flexion (because the flexors now overpower the absent intrinsics at the IP joints). This is more pronounced for digits 4 & 5 in an ulnar claw (ulnar nerve palsy) because digits 2 & 3 still have median-innervated lumbricals. Full intrinsic loss (combined median + ulnar) causes clawing of all four fingers.

7.5 Interossei

There are seven interosseous muscles in total — four dorsal and three palmar — all innervated by the ulnar nerve (deep branch). They occupy the spaces between the metacarpal bones and, like the lumbricals, insert into the extensor hood.

GroupNumberOriginInsertionAction
Dorsal Interossei (DAB) 4 Adjacent sides of two metacarpals (bipennate) Extensor hood + proximal phalanx base ABduction of digits; MCP flexion; IP extension
Palmar Interossei (PAD) 3 Single metacarpal (unipennate) Extensor hood + proximal phalanx base ADduction of digits; MCP flexion; IP extension
Mnemonic DAB = Dorsal ABduct (spread fingers apart)
PAD = Palmar ADduct (bring fingers together)
The axis of abduction/adduction passes through the middle finger — so the middle finger has two dorsal interossei (one on each side) but no palmar interosseus.

8 — The Flexor Pulley System

The flexor tendons (FDS & FDP) to each digit run within a fibro-osseous canal — a tight tunnel formed by the bones below and a series of fibrous pulleys above. Without the pulleys, the tendons would bowstring across the palm when the fingers flex, massively reducing mechanical efficiency.

There are five annular (A) pulleys and three cruciate (C) pulleys in each finger. The annular pulleys are most important clinically:

A1 — MCP Joint Level A2 — Proximal ⅔ of Prox. Phalanx A3 — PIP Joint Level A4 — Middle of Middle Phalanx A5 — DIP Joint Level C1 C2 C3
Most Critical Pulleys A2 and A4 are the most biomechanically critical — they prevent bowstringing at the proximal and middle phalanx respectively. In tendon reconstruction surgery, these two must be preserved or reconstructed at all costs. Loss of A2 alone reduces FDP efficiency by ~30%.
Clinical — Trigger Finger (Stenosing Tenosynovitis) Nodular thickening of the FDP or FDS tendon becomes too large to pass smoothly through the A1 pulley. The finger "clicks" or locks in flexion and has to be manually extended. Most common at the A1 pulley (over MCP joint). Risk factors: diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, repetitive gripping. Treated with corticosteroid injection at the A1 pulley or surgical release.

9 — Innervation & Blood Supply

9.1 Nerve Summary

Three terminal branches of the brachial plexus supply the wrist and hand. Understanding their territory is essential for interpreting sensory loss, weakness, and deformity.

Median Nerve (C6–T1)

  • Enters via carpal tunnel
  • Motor: thenar muscles (APB, OP, FPB superficial head); lateral 2 lumbricals
  • Sensory: palmar skin + dorsal nail beds of lateral 3½ digits
  • Palmar cutaneous branch: thenar skin (exits proximal to tunnel)
  • Lesion at wrist: CTS, thenar wasting, ape hand

Ulnar Nerve (C8–T1)

  • Enters via Guyon's canal (lateral to pisiform)
  • Motor: all hypothenar muscles; medial 2 lumbricals; all interossei; adductor pollicis; FPB deep head
  • Sensory: palmar + dorsal skin of medial 1½ digits
  • Lesion at wrist: ulnar claw (digits 4–5); loss of grip & fine motor

Radial Nerve (C5–T1)

  • Superficial branch enters via anatomical snuffbox
  • Motor to wrist: extensors via PIN (forearm) — no motor branches in the hand itself
  • Sensory: dorsal skin of lateral 3½ digits (to mid-phalanx), anatomical snuffbox, dorsal thumb web
  • Lesion: wrist drop (from forearm); sensory loss on dorsal hand
Innervation Shortcut — LOAF muscles Muscles in the hand supplied by the Median nerve at the wrist: Lumbricals I & II, Opponens Pollicis, Abductor Pollicis Brevis, Flexor Pollicis Brevis (superficial head). Everything else is ulnar. The radial nerve has no intrinsic hand muscles.

9.2 Blood Supply

The hand has a rich, redundant blood supply from both the radial and ulnar arteries, which anastomose to form two palmar arches:

ArchFormed byLocationSupplies
Superficial Palmar Arch Ulnar artery (main contributor) + superficial branch of radial Superficial to flexor tendons, at the level of the extended thumb Common palmar digital arteries → digits 2–5
Deep Palmar Arch Radial artery (main) + deep branch of ulnar (passes between heads of adductor pollicis) Deep to flexor tendons, at the level of the metacarpal bases Palmar metacarpal arteries; anastomoses with superficial arch

The wrist also receives supply from dorsal and palmar carpal arches (anastomoses of radial and ulnar branches at wrist level) — these supply the carpal bones, including the retrograde supply to the scaphoid (via dorsal carpal branch of the radial artery).
