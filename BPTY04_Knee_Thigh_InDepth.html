1. Tibiofemoral Joint — Design and Mechanics

Slide 3 – Knee joint overview

The knee is typically described as a "modified hinge" — accurate but insufficient. It is the largest and most complex synovial joint in the body, operating as a hinge through most of its arc but incorporating axial rotation in a precisely controlled pattern that is essential for stability. This combination of demands — high mobility in one plane, controlled rotation, and enormous compressive loads — is what makes the knee both mechanically sophisticated and clinically vulnerable.

The tibiofemoral joint articulates the two large femoral condyles with the tibial plateau. The medial femoral condyle is larger and its arc of curvature changes more than the lateral condyle — it has a longer articular surface anteroposteriorly, which is what drives the screw-home mechanism. The tibial plateau is not flat: the medial plateau is slightly concave and larger; the lateral plateau is convex and slightly smaller. Without the menisci, these surfaces would be incongruent.

The Screw-Home Mechanism — why the knee "locks":
As the knee approaches full extension (from approximately 20° to 0°), the tibia automatically externally rotates on the femur — typically 5–10° in open kinetic chain. This arises because the medial femoral condyle has a longer articular path to traverse than the lateral, so the lateral side finishes its roll first, pulling the tibia into ER to complete extension. The ACL's tension and the geometry of the femoral condyles both contribute.

This rotational "locking" congruently packs the joint into its most stable, close-packed position — ligaments are taut, menisci are compressed and seated, and the knee requires minimal muscular energy to maintain. To flex from the locked position, popliteus must first "unlock" the knee by internally rotating the tibia (approximately 5° of tibial IR), which is why popliteus has been described as "the key that unlocks the knee."

Clinical relevance: ACL deficiency disrupts the coupled external rotation that achieves close-packing, producing rotatory instability (the pivot shift) that is more disabling than simple anteroposterior laxity.

Knee Joint Reaction Forces

The tibiofemoral joint reaction force depends enormously on the activity and knee angle. During normal walking it reaches approximately 2–3× bodyweight. During stair climbing, 3–4× BW. During deep squatting, 7–8× BW. This understanding underpins exercise prescription in rehabilitation: loading the knee through deeper ranges dramatically increases joint reaction forces, which may be appropriate in later stage rehab but contraindicated early after injury or surgery.

2. Menisci — Load Management and Injury

Slide 4 – Meniscal anatomy and zones

The menisci are among the most important structures in the knee — not just for load distribution, but for the entire mechanical integrity of the joint. Understanding why they work the way they do, and what happens when they fail, is essential for clinical reasoning around meniscal pathology.

The Mechanics of Load Distribution

Under compressive load, the femoral condyle wants to push the meniscus outward radially. The circumferentially oriented collagen fibres of the meniscus (hoop fibres) resist this, converting compressive force into tensile hoop stress around the circumference — like a compressed barrel hoop. This allows the meniscus to distribute force over a much wider articular surface area.

In quantitative terms: with menisci intact, the tibiofemoral contact area per condyle is approximately 6 cm², distributing 30–70% of compressive loads across the joint. After total meniscectomy, contact area drops to approximately 2 cm² per condyle — a three-fold reduction — concentrating the same force on a much smaller area of cartilage. This dramatically accelerates cartilage wear, which is why meniscal preservation (repair over resection) is now the surgical preference wherever possible.

Why the medial meniscus tears more often than the lateral:
The medial meniscus is significantly less mobile than the lateral. It is firmly tethered peripherally to the MCL (via the coronary ligaments), the posterior capsule, and directly to the medial tibial plateau. The lateral meniscus, by contrast, has no collateral ligament attachment and is more loosely attached — it can move up to 11 mm anteroposteriorly during knee flexion (compared to ~5 mm medially). When the knee is loaded in flexion and rotation, the meniscus must move to accommodate the femoral condyle — if it cannot escape quickly enough (as is the case medially), it is caught and torn.

Meniscal Vascularity and Healing Potential

The peripheral 25–30% of each meniscus (the red zone) is supplied by branches of the geniculate arteries and has healing potential after surgical repair. The inner 70–75% (the white zone) is completely avascular, dependent on synovial diffusion for nutrition, and cannot heal independently. This zonal distinction is the primary determinant of surgical decision-making: tears in the red zone in young, active patients are candidates for repair; white zone tears are typically managed with partial meniscectomy, accepting the long-term cartilage consequences.

Meniscal tears — types, tests, and management logic:
Acute tears (typically younger patients, twisting or squatting mechanism): bucket-handle tears cause mechanical blocking ("locking" — inability to fully extend); radial tears disrupt hoop stress function. McMurray (joint-line pain + click in rotation) and Thessaly (weight-bearing rotation at 20° flexion) have moderate diagnostic accuracy; MRI has ~93% sensitivity and ~79% specificity for meniscal tears.

Degenerative tears (typically >40 years, no clear mechanism, associated with OA): randomised trials including the landmark METEOR, FIDELITY, and ESCAPE trials have consistently shown that physiotherapy is as effective as arthroscopic partial meniscectomy for degenerative tears with or without mild OA. Surgery for degenerative tears is no longer first-line in most clinical guidelines. Exercise therapy — particularly quadriceps and hip strengthening — is the evidence-based first treatment.

Collateral Ligaments — Valgus and Varus Control

The MCL and LCL are not simply "medial and lateral stays." Each has distinct anatomy and a specific mechanical role, and each contributes differently across the arc of knee motion.

The MCL (medial collateral / tibial collateral ligament) is a broad, flat structure with two layers: the superficial MCL (primary restraint, resists valgus at all angles) and the deep MCL (shorter fibres blending with the capsule and medial meniscus via coronary ligaments). Crucially, the medial meniscus is attached to the deep MCL — a valgus injury that tears the MCL often simultaneously damages the medial meniscus through this anatomical tethering. MCL tears heal well conservatively due to good vascularity.

The LCL (lateral collateral / fibular collateral ligament) is a round, cord-like structure entirely separate from the lateral capsule and the lateral meniscus (unlike the MCL-medial meniscus relationship). It is taut only in extension, so isolated LCL injuries are less common. When combined with posterolateral capsular injury, it forms part of the critical posterolateral corner (LCL + popliteus + arcuate popliteal ligament + popliteofibular ligament).

Cruciate Ligaments — The Anteroposterior and Rotatory Governors

Both cruciates are intracapsular but extrasynovial — they lie inside the joint capsule but are wrapped in synovial tissue, meaning they have their own blood supply from the middle geniculate artery. This extravascular location partially explains why ACL ruptures do not haemarthrosis immediately (blood collects in the joint space but the ligament itself is not directly perfused by the joint cavity).

ACL Rupture — epidemiology, mechanism, and sex differences:
ACL rupture is one of the most devastating sporting injuries, with approximately 200,000 injuries per year in the US alone, and recovery requiring 9–12 months of rehabilitation. Approximately 70% of ACL ruptures occur via non-contact mechanisms — landing from a jump, decelerating suddenly, or pivoting — rather than direct contact.

Female athletes have 2–8× higher ACL injury risk than males in the same sports (soccer, basketball, handball). Multiple factors contribute: wider Q-angle (increases valgus stress), smaller ACL cross-sectional area, greater ligamentous laxity (hormonally modulated, particularly around ovulation), landing biomechanics (greater dynamic valgus, reduced hip and knee flexion at contact), and neuromuscular patterns (slower hamstring pre-activation relative to quads). Importantly, these are modifiable biomechanical factors — prevention programmes targeting landing mechanics and hip strength reduce ACL injury rates by 50–60%.

Return to sport: Time-based criteria alone (9 months) are insufficient — strength-based criteria (quadriceps limb symmetry index ≥90%, hamstring LSI ≥90%), hop test performance, and psychological readiness (ACL-RSI score) should all be achieved before clearance.

Posterior Capsular Structures and the Popliteal Fossa

The posterior capsule is reinforced by two important ligaments: the oblique popliteal ligament (OPL — an expansion of the semimembranosus tendon sweeping superolaterally across the posterior capsule, resisting hyperextension) and the arcuate popliteal ligament (from the fibular head, arching over the popliteus muscle). Together with the LCL and popliteus, the arcuate ligament forms the posterolateral corner (PLC), which resists varus, ER, and posterior tibial translation in combination. Isolated PLC injuries are rare; combined ACL + PLC injuries can produce complex multi-directional instability that is difficult to reconstruct.

Popliteal fossa — contents and clinical relevance:
Bordered by: biceps femoris (superolateral), semimembranosus + semitendinosus (superomedial), medial and lateral heads of gastrocnemius (inferior). Contents from medial to lateral: tibial nerve → popliteal vein → popliteal artery (deepest — remember TVA from medial to lateral, or that the artery is most anterior, hugging the popliteal surface of femur). The common peroneal nerve runs along the medial border of biceps femoris and wraps around the fibular neck — the most clinically vulnerable point (peroneal palsy from fibular head fracture, lateral knee injury, or poorly positioned plaster cast).

4. Patellofemoral Joint — Tracking, Biomechanics, and Pain

Slide 14 – Posterior knee / PFJ overview

Slide 15 – Patella and patellofemoral joint

Slide 16 – Extensor mechanism

The patella is the largest sesamoid bone in the human body, and its presence increases the mechanical advantage of the quadriceps for knee extension by approximately 30–50% (by increasing the moment arm of the patellar tendon force through the knee joint). Without the patella, the quadriceps would need proportionally greater force to produce the same extension torque — explaining why patellectomy leads to significant functional quadriceps weakness.

Patellofemoral Contact Mechanics

The posterior surface of the patella has medial and lateral facets, both covered in the thickest hyaline cartilage in the body (up to 6–7 mm). The contact zone between patella and trochlear groove changes systematically with knee angle: at 20° flexion, only the distal patella contacts the trochlea. Contact moves progressively proximally on the patella as flexion increases, reaching the proximal pole at ~90°, and involving the "odd" medial facet only at extreme flexion. This progressive proximal migration means that exercises at lower knee flexion angles (0–40°) concentrate stress on the distal patella — relevant in PFPS management.

Patellofemoral joint reaction force (PFJRF) = the resultant of the quadriceps tendon force and the patellar tendon force. PFJRF increases with both increasing knee flexion and increasing quadriceps force. This explains why squatting is particularly painful in patellofemoral disorders — it combines high flexion angle with high quadriceps demand.

Q-Angle — clinical utility and limitations:
The Q-angle measures the resultant lateral pull of the quadriceps on the patella. An elevated Q-angle (above ~17° in females, ~14° in males) theoretically increases the lateral component of patellar force, predisposing to maltracking. However, current evidence shows a more nuanced picture:

Only approximately 50% of PFPS patients show objective patellar tracking abnormalities, and the correlation between static Q-angle measurement and dynamic valgus alignment during activities is poor. The Q-angle is more useful as a component of a multifactorial biomechanical assessment than as a standalone predictor. Dynamic assessments — watching the knee during single-leg squat, step descent, or landing — reveal the real functional problem, which is usually a combination of femoral IR (from weak hip ER/ABD) and tibial ER producing the characteristic "squinting patella" pattern in motion.
PFPS — the proximal and distal contributors:
PFPS is the most common cause of anterior knee pain, affecting up to 25% of athletes. The traditional model (VMO weakness → lateral patellar maltracking → pain) is an oversimplification. Current evidence supports a proximal control model:

Females with PFPS consistently demonstrate weakness of hip abductors, external rotators, and extensors. Hip weakness allows femoral IR during loading, which effectively increases the lateral vector of patellofemoral loading without any change in quadriceps activation. This is why hip strengthening (particularly gluteus medius and maximus) is now central to PFPS rehabilitation — and why trials of isolated VMO strengthening alone have had modest outcomes.

The VMO does play a role in preventing lateral patellar tilt via its medially directed pull on the patella, and VMO atrophy is rapid and disproportionate after knee injury. But the VMO cannot function effectively if proximal control is inadequate — treatment must address both. McConnell patellar taping provides short-term pain relief and allows progressive loading; foot orthoses may help if excessive subtalar pronation drives the tibial IR component.

5. Medial Thigh Muscles — Adductors in Context

The medial thigh compartment is frequently lumped together as "the adductors," but this misses the functional diversity of the group. Adductor magnus in particular is architecturally unusual — its ischial (hamstring) part functions entirely as a hip extensor, while its pubic part functions as an adductor. This dual identity means that the "adductor" compartment includes a significant hip extensor, a knee flexor (gracilis), and a biarticular hip stabiliser (sartorius).

Pes Anserinus — "Say Grace before Tea":
Sartorius · Gracilis · Semitendinosus — insert together at the medial proximal tibia in this anterior-to-posterior order, forming the "goose's foot" (pes anserinus). Their common bursa can be inflamed (pes anserinus bursitis) — particularly in obese patients and those with medial compartment OA, where the added valgus stress increases medial soft tissue compression.

Together, the pes anserinus muscles all contribute tibial IR and knee flexion — making them important dynamic valgus control structures during single-leg activities.
Adductor/Groin Strain — anatomy informs management:
The adductor longus origin at the pubic body is the most common site of acute groin strain, particularly in sports requiring explosive kicking, sprinting, and direction changes. The muscle's line of pull at the pubic attachment means that powerful hip ADD from an abducted position creates a combined tensile and torsional load at the origin — the typical mechanism.

Chronic adductor-related groin pain is distinct and overlaps with rectus abdominis-related pain (since both attach near the pubis). The Copenhagen adductor programme (a progressive eccentric adductor strengthening protocol) has strong evidence for reducing groin strain incidence — by approximately 40% in randomised trials in professional football. Outcome is strongly predicted by adductor squeeze test strength and eccentric strength symmetry.

6. Quadriceps — Extension, Inhibition, and Rehab

The quadriceps femoris is the largest muscle mass in the body (by volume) and the definitive functional muscle of the knee. All four heads converge on the quadriceps tendon, which passes over the patella (with the patella embedded within it) and continues as the patellar tendon to insert on the tibial tuberosity. All are innervated by the femoral nerve (L2–L4).

Arthrogenic Muscle Inhibition (AMI) — why the quad disappears after knee injury:
Even modest knee joint effusion produces a well-documented reflex inhibition of the quadriceps, mediated by mechanoreceptors in the joint capsule and ligaments that send inhibitory signals to quadriceps motor neurones via the spinal cord. This is not a voluntary or pain-mediated response — it is a direct neurological reflex.

The effect is substantial: as little as 20–30 mL of intra-articular fluid can reduce quadriceps activation by 50–60%, and the VMO is inhibited more than the other heads. This explains the dramatic quad wasting seen after ACL rupture, meniscal tears, or TKR, and why patients struggle to perform a straight-leg raise in the acute phase. Early aspiration of large effusions, cryotherapy to reduce swelling, and neuromuscular electrical stimulation (NMES) all partially mitigate AMI. If AMI is not addressed, rehabilitation stalls regardless of willpower.

7. Hamstrings — Injury, Prevention, and Evidence

The hamstrings represent one of the most clinically and biomechanically important muscle groups in sport. They are simultaneously the most commonly injured muscle group in football codes, and the most functionally critical deceleration and knee stabilisation system in the lower limb. Understanding them requires moving beyond origin-insertion-action and engaging with their architecture, neural supply, and the evidence around injury and prevention.

Rotational contributions — more than just flexion:
The differential ER vs IR action of the lateral vs medial hamstrings is clinically critical. Biceps femoris externally rotates the tibia — it can counteract the internal rotation force during the screw-home mechanism and resist valgus collapse. The medial hamstrings (semitend + semimemb) internally rotate the tibia — they contribute to the dynamic stabilisation of the medial compartment, complementing the MCL. Training all three hamstrings, not just total hamstring strength, is important for multi-plane knee stability.
Hamstring-to-Quadriceps (H:Q) Ratio — interpreting it correctly:
The conventional H:Q ratio (peak concentric hamstring torque ÷ peak concentric quad torque) is typically ~0.6–0.8 at 60°/s. Values below this threshold are associated with increased ACL injury risk because the hamstrings are insufficient to decelerate anterior tibial shear during landing. However, a more functionally relevant measure is the functional H:Q ratio: eccentric hamstring torque ÷ concentric quadriceps torque — reflecting the real-world scenario where the hamstrings decelerate eccentrically while the quads push concentrically. This ratio should ideally exceed 1.0 (hamstrings eccentrically stronger than quads concentrically), particularly for return-to-sport decisions.
Nordic Hamstring Exercise — what the evidence actually shows:
The Nordic Hamstring Exercise (NHE) is the most studied single exercise for hamstring strain prevention. Meta-analyses show it reduces hamstring injury incidence by approximately 51% when implemented across a full season. The mechanisms are structural: NHE drives serial sarcomere addition and increases biceps femoris long head fascicle length — shorter fascicles (<10.56 cm preseason) are associated with 4× greater injury risk; a 11% increase in fascicle length reduces injury probability by ~21%.

However, an important nuance has emerged: the NHE primarily activates the semitendinosus, not the biceps femoris long head — despite BFLH being the most commonly injured hamstring. Exercises that more effectively target BFLH include the loaded single-leg Roman Chair Hold (hip extension loaded at length) and hip extension exercises at long muscle lengths. The implication is that the NHE should be supplemented, not replaced, with exercises specifically loading the BFLH eccentrically at long lengths.

8. ITB and Lateral Structures

Slide 9 – Other lateral structures

Slide 10 – Iliotibial band

The ITB is not simply a muscle tendon — it is a specialised thickening of the deep fascia of the thigh that serves multiple mechanical functions. Understanding this is important because it explains both the ITB's functional role in normal gait and the mechanism by which it becomes painful in ITBS.

The ITB receives insertions from two key muscles — the gluteus maximus (upper fibres predominantly) and the TFL (whose entire muscle belly inserts into the ITB). The combined force of these two muscles is transmitted through the ITB to Gerdy's tubercle on the lateral tibia, providing a powerful hip abduction and extension moment during stance. The ITB also stabilises the knee against varus collapse in early stance, particularly important in running where single-limb loading is extreme.

ITB Syndrome — mechanism reconsidered:
The traditional "friction" model of ITBS (the ITB rubbing over the lateral femoral epicondyle) has been largely replaced by a compression model. The ITB does not slide over the epicondyle — it moves anteriorly and posteriorly relative to the femur, but there is a richly innervated fat pad and bursa beneath the ITB at the lateral femoral epicondyle. At approximately 30° of knee flexion (the "impingement zone"), the ITB compresses this tissue against the femoral epicondyle, producing pain.

Risk factors: running volume increase (too much too soon), hip abductor weakness (increasing lateral knee loading), excessive downhill running, narrow stance width (increases tibial adduction moment), worn footwear.

Management: Load reduction (reduce running mileage) is essential first. Hip abductor and external rotator strengthening reduces lateral loading. Running gait retraining — increasing step width and reducing contralateral pelvic drop — directly unloads the lateral knee. Foam rolling the ITB provides temporary relief but does not address the underlying cause; static passive ITB stretching also has limited evidence. In refractory cases, corticosteroid injection into the subITB bursa reduces inflammation acutely.
