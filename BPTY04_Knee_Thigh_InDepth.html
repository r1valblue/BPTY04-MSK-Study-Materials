1. Tibiofemoral Joint Overview

Slide 3 – Overview of the knee joint

The knee is the body's largest and most complex synovial joint. It is primarily classified as a modified hinge (ginglymus) joint, but it also permits a small degree of rotation — critical for the screw-home mechanism that locks the knee into full extension.

The tibiofemoral joint articulates the two large femoral condyles with the relatively flat tibial plateau. The medial condyle is larger and more convex; the lateral condyle is smaller and rounder. The tibial plateau is slightly concave medially but flat-to-convex laterally, and is deepened by the menisci to increase joint congruency.

Screw-Home Mechanism:
As the knee approaches full extension, the tibia externally rotates on the femur (~5–10° in open chain) to lock the knee. This is driven by the asymmetric shape of the femoral condyles (medial is longer) and the pull of the ACL and popliteus. Popliteus "unlocks" the knee by internally rotating the tibia at the onset of flexion. Failure of this mechanism (e.g., from ACL insufficiency) can cause rotatory instability.

2. Menisci

Slide 4 – Menisci

The medial and lateral menisci are crescent-shaped fibrocartilaginous discs that rest on the tibial plateau, attached at their anterior and posterior horns and peripherally to the capsule. They are intracapsular but extrasynovial.

Functions of the Menisci

  • Load distribution: transmit ~60–70% of compressive loads in extension and ~85–90% in flexion; increase contact area, reducing articular cartilage stress
  • Shock absorption: radially oriented collagen fibres absorb compressive loads and convert them to hoop stress (circumferential fibres)
  • Joint stabilisation: particularly the medial meniscus, which acts as a secondary restraint to anterior tibial translation in ACL-deficient knees
  • Lubrication and nutrition: aid distribution of synovial fluid across the articular surfaces

Blood Supply and Healing

The peripheral 25–30% of each meniscus (the red zone) receives blood supply from the geniculate arteries and is capable of healing after repair. The inner 70–75% (white zone) is avascular and relies on synovial diffusion for nutrition — tears here have poor healing potential and are often treated with partial meniscectomy.

Meniscal Tears:
Acute tears typically occur with twisting mechanisms on a loaded knee (bucket-handle, radial, complex tears). Degenerative tears in older patients relate to cumulative load and OA changes. Clinical tests: McMurray, Thessaly, Apley. MRI gold standard. Management: physiotherapy (degenerative) vs arthroscopic repair/meniscectomy (acute, mechanical symptoms).
Unhappy Triad (O'Donoghue):
Valgus contact injury (e.g., tackle to lateral knee) tears ACL + MCL + medial meniscus. Medial meniscus is involved (not lateral) because it is tethered to the MCL.

Cruciate Ligaments

Both cruciates are intracapsular but extrasynovial (enclosed in a synovial sheath). They cross each other obliquely, providing rotational stability in addition to anterior-posterior restraint.

ACL Rupture:
One of the most common serious knee injuries — non-contact (landing, pivoting) in ~70% of cases, contact mechanism in ~30%. Females have 2–3× higher incidence (multifactorial: landing mechanics, hormonal effects on ligament laxity, neuromuscular differences). Anterior drawer and Lachman are positive; pivot shift for rotatory instability. Reconstruction (BPTB or hamstring autograft) vs conservative management depending on patient age, activity level, and concomitant injury.

Posterior Capsular Ligaments

The oblique popliteal ligament (extension of semimembranosus tendon) reinforces the posterior capsule against hyperextension. The arcuate popliteal ligament (from fibular head) contributes to posterolateral stability. Together with the LCL and popliteus, these form the posterolateral corner — crucial for preventing varus and external rotational instability.

Popliteal Fossa contents: Bounded by hamstrings and gastrocnemius heads. Contains (medial to lateral): semimembranosus, semitendinosus | popliteal artery + vein | tibial nerve | common peroneal nerve | biceps femoris. Also: small saphenous vein, posterior cutaneous nerve of thigh.

4. Patellofemoral Joint (PFJ)

Slide 15 – Patella and patellofemoral joint

Slide 16 – Extensor mechanism

The patella is the largest sesamoid bone in the body, embedded within the quadriceps tendon. Its posterior surface articulates with the femoral trochlear groove. The patella transmits and redirects quadriceps forces, increasing the mechanical advantage of knee extension by ~30%.

Patellar Tracking

Patellar tracking is the path the patella takes within the trochlear groove through the arc of knee flexion-extension. The lateral trochlear groove is more prominent, providing passive lateral stability. Active medial stability is provided by the VMO (vastus medialis oblique fibres) and the MPFL (medial patellofemoral ligament), which is the primary restraint to lateral patellar translation (up to 60% of passive medial restraint).

Q-Angle:
Angle between the line of quadriceps pull (ASIS → centre patella) and the patellar tendon (centre patella → tibial tuberosity). Normal: ~14° in males, ~17° in females. Elevated Q-angle (femoral anteversion, tibial ER, genu valgum, lateral tibial tuberosity) increases lateral patellar stress. Used clinically but has limited diagnostic precision alone.
Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (PFPS) & Patellar Dislocation:
PFPS: anterior knee pain without structural damage — multifactorial (overuse, patellar maltracking, biomechanical). Treat with VMO + hip strengthening, patellar taping, activity modification.
Patellar dislocation: typically lateral, following an awkward landing or valgus load. MPFL torn in virtually all first-time dislocations. Recurrence ~30–40%. Acute: reduction, MPFL assessment. Recurrent: MPFL reconstruction ± tibial tubercle osteotomy.

Pes Anserinus — "Say Grace before Tea":
Sartorius · Gracilis · Semitendinosus — all insert at the medial proximal tibia. The shared bursa here can become inflamed (pes anserinus bursitis) in overweight individuals and those with medial knee OA.
Groin Strain:
Acute overload of the adductor longus (most commonly) at or near the musculotendinous junction. Common in soccer (kicking, rapid direction changes). Presents with medial thigh pain reproduced by resisted ADD. Classify by grade (I–III). Rehab: Copenhagen adductor programme reduces recurrence by ~40%.

6. Anterior Thigh — Quadriceps Group

Slide 23 – Rectus femoris

Slide 24 – Vastus intermedius

Slide 25 – Vastus medialis

The quadriceps femoris is the prime mover of knee extension. All four heads converge on the quadriceps tendon, which inserts into the base of the patella, and continues distally as the patellar ligament (patellar tendon) to the tibial tuberosity. All are innervated by the femoral nerve (L2–L4).

Quadriceps Inhibition after Knee Injury:
Joint effusion reflexly inhibits the quadriceps (arthrogenic muscle inhibition — particularly VMO). Even small effusions (~20–30 mL) can cause measurable quad weakness. Early aspiration and cryotherapy can partially restore quad activation. Failure to address this explains slow quad recovery post-injury or post-surgery.

7. Posterior Thigh — Hamstring Group

Slide 27 – Biceps femoris

Slide 28 – Semitendinosus

Slide 29 – Semimembranosus

The three hamstrings are primary knee flexors and hip extensors. All originate from the ischial tuberosity (except the short head of biceps femoris), are innervated by the sciatic nerve, and are the most commonly strained muscle group in sport.

Rotational contributions: Medial hamstrings (semitendinosus + semimembranosus) produce tibial internal rotation with knee flexion. Biceps femoris produces tibial external rotation. This differential activation is important for dynamic rotational knee control and rehabilitation programming.
H:Q Ratio (Hamstring:Quadriceps strength ratio):
Conventional ratio (H:Q) = peak hamstring torque / peak quadriceps torque (isokinetic) — normal ~0.6–0.8 at 60°/s. Functional ratio (H/Q) = eccentric hamstring / concentric quadriceps — more clinically relevant during deceleration. Low H:Q ratio is associated with ACL injury risk and hamstring strain risk. Target H:Q ≥ 0.6 before return to sport.
Hamstring Strain:
Most common muscle injury in football and sprinting. Most frequently at the proximal MTJ of the biceps femoris long head during late swing phase. Grade I: microtrauma; Grade II: partial tear; Grade III: complete rupture (rare). MRI for grading and prognostication. Nordic hamstring exercise (eccentric) reduces injury incidence by ~50% in prevention programmes.

8. ITB & Lateral Thigh Structures

Slide 9 – Other lateral structures

Slide 10 – Iliotibial band

The iliotibial band (ITB) is a thickening of the deep fascia of the thigh (fascia lata). It receives the majority of the gluteus maximus tendon and the entire insertion of TFL. It inserts distally at Gerdy's tubercle on the lateral tibial condyle.

FeatureDetail
OriginIliac tubercle (posterior) + thoracolumbar fascia
Key inputsGluteus maximus (upper fibres), TFL (full insertion)
InsertionGerdy's tubercle (lateral tibial condyle)
FunctionTransmits hip ABD + extension + ER forces to tibia; lateral knee stabiliser during stance; controls knee varus tendency
Impingement zoneLateral femoral epicondyle at ~30° knee flexion (transition between ER and IR tension)
ITB Syndrome (ITBS):
Repetitive friction or compression of ITB over the lateral femoral epicondyle during the repetitive knee flexion-extension of running. Pain at ~30° knee flexion ("impingement zone"). Prevalence ~12% of running injuries. Treatment: load management, correction of hip abductor weakness, running gait retraining; avoid passive stretching alone.
